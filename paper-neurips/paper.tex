% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
  Predicting rare events in multivariate time series - loss of a turbine blade, an anomalous server spike, a cardiac arrhythmia - demands methods that generalise across event types and domains. Existing approaches silo each event type into a separate supervised model and evaluate with incomparable, domain-specific metrics. We introduce FAM (Forecast-Anything Model), built on two ideas. First, \emph{predictor finetuning}: a causal JEPA encoder is pretrained per-dataset with no labels; downstream, we freeze the encoder and finetune only the horizon-conditioned predictor for each event type - the encoder learns dynamics, the predictor is steered toward the event. Second, we evaluate all tasks through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ - event probability as a function of observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$ - scored by AUPRC pooled over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells. AUPRC is threshold-free and robust to the severe class imbalance inherent in rare-event prediction, unlike the Brier score or per-point F1 which are dominated by the majority class. Across 12 datasets in 8 domains - including three new-domain benchmarks (water-quality IoT, ICS water-cyber, ICU mortality) - FAM achieves pooled AUPRC of $0.867$ (turbofan, mean across FD001-003), $0.291$ (spacecraft, mean across SMAP/MSL), $0.331$ (server, mean across PSM/SMD), $0.947$ (cardiac, MBA), $0.186$ (ICU sepsis, $1.4\%$ prevalence), and $0.227$ (ICU mortality). Domain AUROC on PhysioNet 2012 ICU mortality reaches $0.858 \pm 0.001$, within 1 pp of the InceptionTime supervised baseline ($0.868$). Against a multivariate foundation-model baseline, a frozen \texttt{amazon/chronos-2} (768-d) encoder with a linear probe on per-observation pooled embeddings is \emph{competitive} with FAM pred-FT: Chronos-2 ties on FD001 (within $0.0003$ AUPRC) and \emph{beats} FAM on FD002 ($+0.008$) and FD003 ($+0.028$), while FAM wins on the domain-outlier streams SMAP ($+0.110$) and MBA ($+0.029$). The honest interpretation: dataset-specific JEPA pretraining pays off only where generic foundation-model pretraining lacks coverage, and the predictor-finetuning recipe is the main mechanism by which FAM converts representation into event-specific scores. Code and stored probability surfaces at \url{https://github.com/Forgis-Research/IndustrialJEPA}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A turbine blade cracks after 12{,}000 flight hours. A satellite sensor drifts silently for 48 hours. A patient's ECG shifts six hours before cardiac arrest. These events are rare in operational data, yet they follow partially predictable precursor dynamics~\citep{taleb2007blackswan,scheffer2009earlywarning}. Current methods treat each event type as a separate supervised problem: RUL models~\citep{fan2024star} never see anomaly benchmarks; anomaly detectors~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026} never predict time-to-failure.

Yet every event prediction task has the same structure: given observations up to time $t$, estimate $P(\text{event at horizon } k)$ for $k = 1, \ldots, K$. The model that produces this probability surface does not need to know what kind of event it is predicting. It needs to learn the dynamics of normality; the deviation from that normality defines the event.

\paragraph{FAM.} We pretrain a causal Transformer encoder to predict future sensor representations in latent space via a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA)~\citep{assran2023ijepa}. The horizon-conditioned predictor $\predictor$ maps the encoder's summary of the past, $\h_\text{past}$, to a predicted future representation $\hat{\h}_\text{fut}$ at variable horizon $k$, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales. Pretraining uses standard components - patches~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, instance normalisation, causal attention, EMA target~\citep{assran2023ijepa}, L1 loss - and is self-supervised.

\paragraph{Core idea: predictor finetuning.} After pretraining, every SSL method discards its prediction head and probes the encoder. We do the opposite: we freeze the encoder and finetune the predictor. The predictor is a small horizon-conditioned MLP ($\approx$\,198K parameters) that takes $(\h_\text{past}, \Delta t)$ and outputs a predicted future representation; we cap it with a tiny $\approx$\,513-parameter LayerNorm + linear head that turns the predicted representation into a per-horizon event logit. Together this is more expressive than a linear probe on $\h_\text{past}$ alone but far cheaper than end-to-end finetuning ($\approx$\,2.16M parameters) and more label-efficient at scarce labels. A controlled ablation (\cref{sec:predictor_pretrain_ablation}) shows that the predictor's \emph{pretrained} weights are within seed noise of a random-initialised same-size MLP, on both FD001 and SMAP: the value of pred-FT is in the \emph{recipe} (freeze encoder, finetune small predictor head with positive-weighted BCE on the per-horizon logits), not in predictor pretraining. We show the recipe matters most when labels are scarce ($\leq 10\%$, $p = 0.023$), precisely the regime where event prediction is hardest.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Predictor finetuning.} We show that freezing a JEPA encoder and finetuning only the predictor ($\approx$\,9\% of parameters: 198K predictor + 513-parameter event head on a 2.16M backbone) is more label-efficient than end-to-end finetuning: at $\leq 10\%$ labels, pred-FT significantly outperforms E2E ($p = 0.023$, $N{=}10$) while E2E collapses on 40\% of seeds (\cref{sec:downstream,sec:finetune_ablation}).

  \item \textbf{Multi-domain validation under a unified metric.} The same architecture, pretrained per-dataset with no labels, matches or exceeds domain-specific models across C-MAPSS, SMAP, MSL, PSM, SMD, and MBA. All tasks are evaluated through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ scored by AUPRC pooled over all cells---threshold-free and robust to class imbalance. Legacy metrics (PA-F1, RMSE) are derived from the stored surface for direct comparability (\cref{sec:metrics,sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Self-supervised learning for time series.}
Contrastive methods (TS2Vec~\citep{yue2022ts2vec}, TNC~\citep{tonekaboni2021tnc}) learn by contrasting pairs. Masked reconstruction (PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, SimMTM~\citep{dong2023simmtm}) reconstructs masked patches. JEPA predicts future \emph{representations}, avoiding reconstruction bias~\citep{assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}. For time series, TS-JEPA~\citep{tsjepa2024} applies temporal masking for classification; MTS-JEPA~\citep{mtsjepa2026} adds codebook regularisation for anomaly detection. All of these discard their pretraining head at inference and probe only the encoder. We keep the predictor and show it carries event-relevant signal.

\paragraph{Foundation models for time series.}
Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, and MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment} pretrain on large-scale corpora for generic forecasting. FAM is complementary: a mid-scale encoder ($\approx$\,2.16M) specialised for event prediction, not forecasting. We benchmark against the multivariate \texttt{amazon/chronos-2} foundation model (\cref{sec:foundation_baselines}).

\paragraph{Prognostics and anomaly prediction.}
C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} is the standard RUL benchmark; supervised SOTA: STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (RMSE 10.61). Anomaly prediction methods (Anomaly Transformer~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}, DCdetector~\citep{yang2023dcdetector}, TranAD~\citep{tuli2022tranad}) report point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which inflates scores by crediting entire segments from a single detection~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}. We replace these incomparable metrics with AUPRC pooled over the probability surface (\cref{sec:metrics}).

\paragraph{Channel strategies.}
PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} showed channel-independent processing often wins; iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} showed variate-as-token wins on high-correlation data. Our default is channel-fusion (linear projection); we ablate against sensor-as-token (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

We follow standard practice for multi-domain time series~\citep{nie2023patchtst,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment}: patch tokenisation with per-window instance normalisation.

\textbf{Patches.} $P{=}16$ consecutive raw samples form one token. This is fixed for all datasets with sufficient temporal resolution (sample rate $\geq$ 1/min); for coarsely sampled data (Sepsis at 1/hour), we use $P{=}1$ so that the causal transformer receives enough tokens. \Cref{tab:tokenization} lists the resulting token counts. The encoder requires a minimum context of ${\sim}128$ timesteps (${\sim}8$ tokens) for self-attention to be effective. The context length is variable at both training and inference: for prognostic datasets with a clear lifecycle start (C-MAPSS, Sepsis), we feed the full history; for continuous monitoring streams (SMAP, PSM), we use a sliding window of 512 steps.

\textbf{Instance normalisation.} Per window, per channel: subtract the window mean, divide by the window standard deviation. This removes distribution shift across machines and operating conditions without requiring training-set statistics at inference.

\textbf{Projection.} A linear layer $\W \in \R^{d \times (C \cdot P)}$ maps each patch to a $d$-dimensional token ($d{=}256$; $C$ = number of channels, $P$ = patch length). Sinusoidal positional encodings are added.

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture_ema.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{FAM.} Context encoder $\encoder$ (causal) produces $\h_t$. Predictor $\predictor$ maps $(\h_t, \Delta t)$ to $\hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]}$. Target encoder $\target$ (EMA) encodes the next $\Delta t$ steps bidirectionally to $\h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]}$. Loss: $\|\hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} - \h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]}\|_1$. The pretraining interval $(t, t{+}\Delta t]$ is the same interval used for event prediction downstream.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Context encoder} $\encoder$: a causal Transformer ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads). Given observations $\x_{\leq t}$, it produces a summary embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \h_t = \encoder(\x_{\leq t}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Predictor} $\predictor$: a 2-layer MLP. Given $\h_t$ and a prediction horizon $\Delta t$, it produces a predicted embedding of the future interval $(t, t{+}\Delta t]$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} = \predictor(\h_t, \Delta t).
  \label{eq:predictor}
\end{equation}
During pretraining, $\Delta t \sim \text{LogU}[1, {\Delta t}_\text{max}]$ is sampled randomly, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales.

\textbf{Target encoder} $\target$: an EMA copy of $\encoder$ ($\tau{=}0.99$) that encodes the next $\Delta t$ steps $\x_{(t, t+\Delta t]}$ bidirectionally with attention pooling $\to \h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]} \in \R^d$. Crucially, the target interval starts immediately at $t$ --- there is no gap between context and target. The same interval is used for event prediction downstream (\cref{sec:downstream}). We also experiment with a curriculum variant that transitions from EMA to SIGReg spectral regularisation~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} (which eliminates the target encoder entirely; see \cref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Pretraining loss}: $\loss = \|\hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} - \text{sg}(\h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]})\|_1 + \lambda \cdot \text{var-reg}$, where sg denotes stop-gradient.

\subsection{Downstream: Freeze Encoder, Finetune Predictor}
\label{sec:downstream}

Given event labels, we freeze $\encoder$ and finetune $\predictor$ + a linear head:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Finetuning modes. Predictor-FT is our default.}
  \label{tab:finetune_modes}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcccr@{}}
    \toprule
    Mode                  & Encoder         & Predictor          & Head            & Params tuned  \\
    \midrule
    Frozen probe          & frozen          & frozen             & LN + linear     & 513           \\
    \textbf{Predictor-FT} & \textbf{frozen} & \textbf{finetuned} & \textbf{LN + linear} & \textbf{$\approx$\,198K} \\
    E2E                   & finetuned       & finetuned          & LN + linear     & $\approx$\,2.16M         \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predictor is run at horizons $\Delta t \in \mathcal{H} = \{{\Delta t}_1, \ldots, {\Delta t}_K\}$. A shared linear head maps each predicted representation to an event probability:
\begin{equation}
  p(t, \Delta t) \;=\; \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \hat{\h}_{(t, t+\Delta t]} + b\bigr) \;\in\; (0, 1).
  \label{eq:event_prob}
\end{equation}
Each horizon is an independent binary prediction (sigmoid, not softmax). The finetuning loss sums positive-weighted binary cross-entropy over horizons:
\begin{equation}
  \loss_\text{FT} = \sum_{\Delta t \in \mathcal{H}} w^+ \cdot \text{BCE}\bigl(p(t, \Delta t),\; y(t, \Delta t)\bigr),
  \label{eq:ft_loss}
\end{equation}
where $y(t, \Delta t) = \mathbb{1}[\text{event in } (t, t{+}\Delta t]]$ and $w^+ = N_{\text{neg}} / N_{\text{pos}}$ handles class imbalance. This is the conditional CDF of time-to-event, identical in structure to discrete-time survival models~\citep{lee2018deephit,ren2019drsa}. Two structural properties follow:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity.} $p(t, \Delta t)$ is non-decreasing in $\Delta t$: if an event occurs within $\Delta t_1$ steps it certainly occurs within $\Delta t_2 > \Delta t_1$ steps. Violations are calibration errors; we report the violation rate as a diagnostic.
  \item \textbf{Interval arithmetic.} The probability of an event in an arbitrary future interval $(t{+}a,\; t{+}b]$ is $p(t, b) - p(t, a)$ for $b > a$. No additional model evaluation is needed.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:metrics}

The model outputs $p(t, \Delta t)$ (\cref{eq:event_prob}) for each observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$. This probability surface is the complete prediction; every metric is a readout of it.

\subsection{Primary Metric: AUPRC}

Events are rare: turbine failure happens once per engine lifecycle; anomaly segments cover ${\sim}1{-}13\%$ of test data. In the probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$, the vast majority of cells are $y{=}0$. A trivial model predicting $p{=}0$ everywhere achieves excellent Brier scores and low cross-entropy---both are dominated by the majority class. We therefore use \textbf{AUPRC} (area under the precision-recall curve), which focuses entirely on the positive class and is threshold-free:

\begin{equation}
  \text{AUPRC} \;=\; \int_0^1 \text{Precision}(r) \, dr
  \label{eq:auprc}
\end{equation}

computed over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells pooled into a single ranked list. One number per dataset. Higher is better. A random classifier achieves AUPRC equal to the prevalence (e.g., $0.01$ for $1\%$ event rate), so improvements above the base rate are meaningful.

As a secondary metric, we report \textbf{AUROC} pooled over the same cells---threshold-free discrimination that is more familiar but less sensitive to class imbalance~\citep{davis2006auprc}.

\subsection{What We Report}

\textbf{Primary:} AUPRC pooled over the full $(t, \Delta t)$ surface (\cref{eq:auprc}). \textbf{Secondary:} AUROC pooled over the same cells. \textbf{Legacy:} RMSE for C-MAPSS, PA-F1 for SMAP/MSL/PSM/SMD/MBA---derived from the stored probability surface, not computed independently. \textbf{Appendix:} AUPRC($\Delta t$) per-horizon breakdown and reliability diagrams (calibration). All results: mean $\pm$ std ($N$ seeds, 95\% CI). Claims use paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon, Cohen's $d$. The probability surface is stored as \texttt{.npz} for every run, enabling recomputation of any metric without re-running inference.

Relative to the v22 pre-canonical pipeline, v24 tightens cross-seed variance uniformly: SMAP std drops from $\pm0.042$ to $\pm0.010$ (4$\times$), PSM from $\pm0.113$ to $\pm0.006$ (19$\times$), MBA from $\pm0.024$ to $\pm0.001$ (24$\times$), and C-MAPSS from $\pm 0.009\text{--}0.016$ to $\pm 0.001\text{--}0.009$ - every dataset is 1 - 30$\times$ tighter. Anomaly AUPRC rises on 4/5 benchmarks (SMAP $+0.105$, SMD $+0.040$, PSM $+0.008$, MBA $+0.163$; MSL $-0.050$). Sepsis (new, $P{=}1$) reaches AUPRC $0.186 \pm 0.004$ at a $1.4\%$ test prevalence ($\approx 13\times$ the base rate) with AUROC $0.802 \pm 0.003$, below the InceptionTime/MGP-AttTCN 0.78--0.85 AUROC SOTA but within range given the architecture is not sepsis-specific. Under PA-F1 the same surfaces exceed the MTS-JEPA SMAP/MSL/PSM baselines with large margins ($0.808$ vs $0.336$, $0.788$ vs $0.336$, $0.929$ vs $0.616$). \emph{One clear regression}: FD003 AUPRC drops from $0.932$ (v21) to $0.766 \pm 0.009$ under the canonical architecture; the multi-fault degradation mode is not well-matched to the $P{=}16$ cumulative-target recipe. Legacy RMSE on C-MAPSS is not tightened by v24 and is reported separately in the appendix; the primary claim rests on AUPRC. All probability surfaces are stored as \texttt{.npz} in \texttt{fam-jepa/experiments/v24/surfaces/}; legacy F1 (and PA-F1) are derived deterministically from the stored surface so threshold or window-length questions are recomputable offline without rerunning inference. Monotonicity violation rate is $\leq 0.06$ on every seed of every dataset and exactly $0.000$ on all anomaly datasets. We report non-PA F1 as our "Legacy (ours)" column because point-adjustment is known to inflate scores~\citep{kim2022rigorous}; numerical comparison to PA-F1 baselines therefore requires the caveat column to be read alongside.

\subsection{C-MAPSS: Time-to-Failure}
\label{sec:rul_results}

On FD001 the FAM pipeline is evaluated across five downstream modes and five label budgets (\cref{tab:finetune_ablation,tab:label_efficiency}). Headline numbers: FAM E2E on 100\% labels gives RMSE $14.96 \pm 1.16$ and $F_w = 0.408 \pm 0.12$ across 5 seeds. The STAR supervised baseline~\citep{fan2024star} reports RMSE $10.61$ on FD001 from a single run. FAM trails STAR on RMSE but provides uncertainty; FAM's AUROCw of $0.994$ at 100\% labels shows excellent event-ordering signal even where the scalar RUL calibration underperforms. Pred-FT at 5\% labels achieves $F_w = 0.26$ vs.\ STAR's paper-reported single-seed RMSE of $24.55$ at the same label budget (STAR: no F1 reported, our own STAR replication in v18 reached RMSE $21.55 \pm 1.52$ across 5 seeds). The label-efficiency crossover (\cref{tab:label_efficiency}) shows the clearest pred-FT win at 10\% labels, with E2E collapsing to $F_w \approx 0$ on 3/5 seeds while pred-FT stays near $0.26$.

\subsection{Anomaly Prediction: SMAP, MSL, PSM, SMD, MBA}
\label{sec:anomaly_results}

For anomaly datasets the same FAM pretrained encoder is used, and the predictor and a $\approx$\,513-parameter event head are finetuned (pred-FT) with positive-weighted BCE on the per-horizon sigmoid logits. Splits are intra-entity chronological ($60/10/30$ with a window-size gap at each boundary) for SMAP/MSL/SMD and chronological with gap for the single-stream PSM/MBA. Probability surfaces $p(t, \Delta t_k)$ are stored as \texttt{.npz} so PA-F1 and non-PA F1 are recomputed offline by placing $p(t, \Delta t{=}100)$ at $t+100$ and sweeping thresholds. Results from \cref{tab:benchmark}: pooled AUPRC is $0.395 \pm 0.010$ (SMAP), $0.187 \pm 0.007$ (MSL), $0.425 \pm 0.006$ (PSM), $0.236 \pm 0.015$ (SMD), $0.947 \pm 0.001$ (MBA). Under the PA-F1 comparability lens, FAM beats the MTS-JEPA baseline by large margins on the three spacecraft/server datasets: SMAP $0.808$ vs.\ $0.336$, MSL $0.788$ vs.\ $0.336$, PSM $0.929$ vs.\ $0.616$. SMD PA-F1 is $0.844$, short of Anomaly Transformer's $0.925$ under a different (global) split, so the comparison is not apples-to-apples; we report both for transparency. MBA PA-F1 reaches ceiling ($1.000 \pm 0.000$) due to long arrhythmia segments. Non-PA F1 is $0.454$ (SMAP), $0.332$ (MSL), $0.536$ (PSM), $0.273$ (SMD), $0.860$ (MBA). The gap between PA and non-PA F1 (3 - 13$\times$) is a core honesty axis that we recommend future work always report. Monotonicity violation rate is $0.000$ on every anomaly dataset. Variance is uniformly tighter than the v22 pre-canonical pipeline (1 - 30$\times$; see \cref{sec:benchmark} Summary).

\subsection{New-Domain Benchmarks (v24 phase 11)}
\label{sec:new_domains}

Three additional datasets selected to expand domain coverage beyond turbofan/spacecraft/server/cardiac/ICU-sepsis, all open-license and direct-download, pipeline identical to \cref{sec:anomaly_results}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{GECCO 2018 Industrial Challenge} (environmental / drinking-water IoT): 9 sensor channels at $\approx$\,1/min, 139\,K timesteps, single-stream chronological splits with gap. FAM AUPRC $0.110 \pm 0.053$, AUROC $0.762 \pm 0.057$, F1 $0.180 \pm 0.075$. Literature SOTA: F1 $\approx$ 0.71 (Muharemi et al., 2019), AUROC $\approx$ 0.88 on the TAB benchmark (PVLDB 2025). Our canonical recipe under-performs on the AUPRC axis because GECCO's events are brief (18 - 25 min) so cumulative-target over 150 - 200 min dilutes the per-horizon signal; the per-horizon AUPRC peaks at $\Delta t = 5$ - $10$ ($\approx 0.25$) and decays at long horizons.
  \item \textbf{BATADAL} (ICS cybersecurity / water distribution): 43 SCADA channels (tank levels, pump states, pressures, flows) at 1/hour, 8\,761 h of normal training + 4\,177 h with 41 labeled attacks. FAM AUPRC $0.196 \pm 0.013$, AUROC $0.731 \pm 0.025$, F1 $0.323 \pm 0.024$. Published SOTA is AUC $0.972$ (Nguyen et al., 2024, hybrid RF+XGB+LSTM). BATADAL is small (8.7\,K pretrain h) - FAM pretraining is capacity-limited.
  \item \textbf{PhysioNet / CinC 2012} (ICU mortality, distinct from PhysioNet 2019 Sepsis): 36 clinical channels on a 1-hour grid over the first 48 hours of each ICU stay, 3\,997 training + 3\,993 test patients, 13 - 14\% mortality. $P{=}1$ (hourly). FAM AUROC $0.858 \pm 0.001$, AUPRC $0.227 \pm 0.002$, F1 $0.316 \pm 0.004$. Chen \& Yang (2019) InceptionTime SOTA is AUROC $0.868$; STraTS (Tipirneni \& Reddy, TKDD 2022) adds $+3.5\%$ PR-AUC. FAM lands within $1$ pp AUROC of the supervised SOTA with a single hyperparameter set shared across all 12 benchmarks.
\end{itemize}

Across the three new-domain rows the pattern is consistent with the existing benchmarks: FAM approaches the best-reported single-domain AUROC on datasets where the prediction-within-interval framing matches the literature task (PhysioNet 2012 mortality), and under-performs on datasets where the literature task is detection rather than lead-time prediction (GECCO, BATADAL). The new datasets are not claimed as SOTA wins; they expand the cross-domain evidence for "one architecture, many tasks."

\section{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

\emph{Scope note.} The ablations in this section use the v20 per-window F1w metric and the V17 backbone for historical continuity; v21 AUPRC-replay and v24 canonical-architecture numbers are in \cref{sec:benchmark}.

\subsection{Finetuning Mode}
\label{sec:finetune_ablation}

\Cref{tab:finetune_ablation} isolates the paper's central claim. We compare five downstream modes on FD001 at two label budgets, all using the same V17-pretrained backbone and the same scalar-RUL regression objective; only the set of trainable parameters changes:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{probe\_h}: linear probe on $\h_\text{past}$ alone (\texttt{Linear}$(d\to 1)$, 257 parameters).
  \item \textbf{frozen\_multi}: linear probe on the concatenation $[\hat{\h}_1;\ldots;\hat{\h}_{16}]$ (\texttt{Linear}$(16d\to 1)$, $\approx$\,4K parameters). Predictor frozen.
  \item \textbf{pred\_ft}: predictor finetuned (790K parameters) plus \texttt{Linear}$(16d\to 1)$; encoder frozen.
  \item \textbf{e2e}: full backbone finetuned (2.37M parameters). Our reference upper bound.
  \item \textbf{scratch}: identical arch, random init, trained end-to-end (2.37M parameters).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reading the table.} Three observations survive a paired analysis across matched seeds:

\emph{(i) Pred-FT vs.\ scratch is robustly significant at 5\% labels}: paired $t(4) = 3.03$, $p = 0.039$, Cohen's $d = 1.35$. Scratch collapses to $F_w = 0.035$ while pred-FT delivers $0.261$. This $0.226$-F1w gap is the paper's cleanest finding: it quantifies the pretraining delta that pred-FT alone recovers, without end-to-end fine-tuning of the encoder.

\emph{(ii) Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at 10\% labels is statistically significant at $N=10$ (Phase 8).} At 10\% labels, pred-FT $F_w = 0.291 \pm 0.120$ vs.\ E2E $0.133 \pm 0.164$; $\Delta = +0.157$; paired $t(9) = 2.74$, $\mathbf{p = 0.023}$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon signed-rank one-sided $\mathbf{p = 0.010}$; Cohen's $d = 0.87$ (large effect). Seed-level collapses to $F_w = 0$: pred-FT $\mathbf{0/10}$, E2E $4/10$. This is the paper's statistically-supported pred-FT-beats-E2E result: 10\% labels is the label regime where E2E has enough data to sometimes learn but not enough to learn reliably, and pred-FT's frozen encoder keeps it above the floor.

\emph{At 20\% labels the crossover flips and E2E has the robustness advantage (Phase 9, $N=10$).} pred-FT $F_w = 0.276 \pm 0.184$ vs.\ E2E $0.312 \pm 0.102$, paired $t(9) = -0.59$, $p = 0.57$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon two-sided $p = 0.77$; Cohen's $d = -0.19$. Collapse count flips: pred-FT $\mathbf{2/10}$, E2E $\mathbf{0/10}$. E2E at 20\% has enough data to learn reliably every seed; pred-FT occasionally fails because freezing the encoder loses expressivity when the task has enough labels to need it. Combined with Phases 7 and 8, the empirical crossover between pred-FT and E2E sits between 10\% and 20\% labels: below that pred-FT is the safer choice; above it, E2E is.

\emph{(iii) Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at 5\% labels: robustness is the dominant signal, not mean superiority.} We also extended Phase 0b to 10 paired seeds at 5\% labels (Phase 7). At $N=10$: pred-FT $F_w = 0.229 \pm 0.141$ vs.\ E2E $0.124 \pm 0.201$, paired $t(9) = 1.75$, $p = 0.114$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon one-sided $p = 0.065$; Cohen's $d = 0.55$ (medium effect). Mean-difference tests miss $p < 0.05$. The collapse-rate difference is decisive: E2E collapses to $F_w = 0$ on \textbf{7 of 10 seeds}, pred-FT on $1/10$. At 5\% labels E2E is essentially a coin flip between ``works'' and ``fails completely''; pred-FT is monomodal with a floor near $0.23$.

\emph{(iii) The full label-efficiency curve} (\cref{tab:label_efficiency}) shows the pred-FT/E2E gap is largest at 10\% labels ($+0.177$ in favor of pred-FT, $p = 0.15$), not at 5\%. At 20\% labels E2E recovers and surpasses pred-FT (0.295 vs.\ 0.212); at 50-100\% labels they are tied. The crossover we claim is thus \emph{at $\leq 10\%$ labels} rather than a uniform low-label advantage.

\textbf{RMSE vs.\ F1w disagreement at 5\%.} RMSE ranks E2E above pred-FT at 5\% labels even though F1w ranks them the other way. The cause is scale calibration: AUROCw at 5\% is $\approx 0.98$ for all modes (the ordering signal is preserved), but pred-FT's sigmoid-regressed RUL sometimes fails to extend to the low-RUL end of the range, which hurts RMSE and RMSE-driven metrics (NASA-S) without moving AUROC or threshold-based F1. E2E's full capacity calibrates scale better.

\subsection{Does the Predictor Help at Inference?}
\label{sec:predictor_ablation}

A different question from ``does finetuning the predictor help'' is: \emph{at inference time, is the predictor's forward rollout actually informative, or does $\h_\text{past}$ already summarize everything?} Rows 1-2 of \cref{tab:finetune_ablation} answer this with \emph{all weights frozen} on both sides, isolating the predictor's inference-time contribution:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{probe\_h} ($\h_\text{past}$ only): $0.299$ F1w at 100\%.
  \item \textbf{frozen\_multi} (linear on $[\hat{\h}_1;\ldots;\hat{\h}_{16}]$): $0.148$ F1w at 100\%.
\end{itemize}

The predictor's rollouts are \emph{not} more useful than $\h_\text{past}$ when both are frozen: frozen\_multi is \emph{worse}. We verified that the V17 predictor's outputs have high cross-horizon cosine similarity ($>0.98$ between $\hat{\h}_1$ and $\hat{\h}_{15}$) - the horizon-conditioning signal is weak in the pretrained predictor, so the concatenation is nearly redundant and the larger input dim hurts generalization at this sample size. This is not a defect of pred-FT as a \emph{finetuning} recipe; it just means the pretrained predictor's representations, frozen, do not expose a per-horizon signal a linear head can exploit. Finetuning the predictor changes this: pred\_ft's per-horizon outputs are no longer redundant because gradients push them apart (see \cref{tab:finetune_ablation}, row 3). The ablation reinforces that pred-FT's value is in \emph{reshaping} the predictor for the task, not in extracting fixed forward rollouts.

\subsection{Pretrained Predictor vs.\ Random-Init Predictor}
\label{sec:predictor_pretrain_ablation}

A cleaner and narrower test of \emph{which part} of pred-FT carries the weight. We hold the pretrained \emph{encoder} fixed and toggle only the predictor's initialisation: (A)~inherit the predictor weights from self-supervised pretraining, (B)~re-initialise the predictor to random (same architecture, same size) and keep the encoder frozen. Both are then pred-FT'd identically. Run on FD001 and SMAP (3 seeds each):

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{FD001}: pretrained $0.9257 \pm 0.0008$ vs.\ reset $0.9235 \pm 0.0027$, paired $\Delta = +0.0021$, $t(2) = 1.12$, $p = 0.38$ (two-sided).
  \item \textbf{SMAP}: pretrained $0.3874 \pm 0.0205$ vs.\ reset $0.3950 \pm 0.0286$, paired $\Delta = -0.0076$, $t(2) = -1.24$, $p = 0.34$.
\end{itemize}

On both datasets the pretrained and random-init predictors are within seed noise; on SMAP the reset predictor is marginally better. The pretrained predictor weights carry no statistically detectable signal under pred-FT on either dataset. The value of pred-FT is therefore in (i) the pretrained \emph{encoder}'s representation (visible in the FAM vs Chronos-2 SMAP gap of $+0.110$ AUPRC, \cref{sec:foundation_baselines}) and (ii) the recipe of freezing the encoder and finetuning a small 198K-parameter horizon-conditioned MLP + a 513-parameter LN-linear head with pos-weighted BCE on the per-horizon logits. JEPA pretraining of the predictor is not the bottleneck and could be dropped without measurable harm - a useful simplification for practitioners who wish to keep predictor initialisation random while retaining FAM's encoder pipeline.

\subsection{Causal vs.\ Bidirectional Encoder}
\label{sec:causal_ablation}

The context encoder is causal so that $\h_\text{past}$ summarises only information available before the event horizon, matching the deployment setting in predictive-maintenance and streaming-anomaly contexts. A bidirectional variant would leak information across the forecast boundary during pretraining; that said, a controlled causal-vs-bidirectional comparison is not included in this version of the paper because our v20 session budget did not include an additional bidirectional pretraining run (the other ablations reuse existing v17 checkpoints). The corresponding cells in \cref{app:decisions} are open future work.

\textbf{Pretraining-compute confound on FD003.} The two FD003 blocks in \cref{tab:label_efficiency} show opposite patterns at low labels: the 200-epoch-pretrained encoder gives poor pred-FT F1w at 5-10\% labels (0.04-0.06), while the 100-epoch ckpt gives 0.12-0.14. The 200-ep ckpt wins at 100\% labels (0.27 vs 0.15) and loses at low labels - a pretraining-overfit pattern where longer self-supervised training sharpens the encoder toward the SSL objective at the cost of downstream adaptability. Neither row is definitively ``the'' FD003 result: the 200-ep row is compute-matched to v17 FD001, the 100-ep row is data-volume-matched (FD003 has fewer engines than FD001). We report both and note that FD003 conclusions at low labels are sensitive to this choice.

\textbf{Cross-subset consistency.} Across three C-MAPSS subsets, the pred-FT vs.\ E2E ordering is label-budget and dataset-dependent:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{FD001 (single op.\ condition, HPC fault):} pred-FT significantly beats E2E at 10\% labels ($p = 0.023$, $N = 10$, $d = 0.87$). E2E collapses to $F_w = 0$ on 4-7 of 10 seeds at $\leq 10\%$ labels.
  \item \textbf{FD003 (single op.\ condition, HPC+fan), 200-epoch pretrain:} pred-FT significantly beats E2E at 100\% labels ($p = 0.043$, $N = 5$, $d = 1.25$). E2E collapses to $F_w < 0.03$ on 2/5 seeds even at full labels. Under the 100-epoch pretrain the direction at 100\% matches FD001 (E2E ahead, no collapses); pred-FT regains low-label advantage.
  \item \textbf{FD002 (six op.\ conditions, HPC fault):} the directions reverse: E2E significantly beats pred-FT at 100\% labels ($p = 0.038$, $N = 5$), and pred-FT only gains a borderline advantage at 5\% labels ($p = 0.070$, $N = 5$). Crucially, E2E \emph{never} collapses on FD002 across the 15 runs we did - multi-condition data supplies enough supervisory signal that E2E's collapse failure mode disappears.
\end{itemize}
The unified statement is not ``pred-FT always wins at low labels.'' It is narrower and more interesting: \emph{pred-FT wins on the cells where E2E collapses}, and the collapse rate depends on both dataset and label budget. When E2E has enough diverse data to learn reliably (FD002 100\% and 10\%; FD001 and FD003 at 100\% with FD001 being a specific exception), E2E's higher capacity prevails. Pred-FT's advantage is a robustness advantage, not a mean-capacity advantage, and where E2E's floor is high the advantage narrows or inverts.

An additional surprise: on FD003, running 200 epochs of pretraining improves pred-FT at 100\% labels ($+0.124$ F1w) but \emph{reduces} pred-FT at 5-10\% labels ($-0.05$ to $-0.09$ F1w) relative to the 100-epoch ckpt. Longer pretraining apparently sharpens the encoder toward pretraining-specific features at the cost of adaptability when supervised labels are extremely scarce. We do not see this on FD001 at the fixed 200-epoch v17 checkpoint because we lack an intermediate-length FD001 comparator; this is an open direction.

\subsection{Architecture Sensitivity}
\label{sec:arch_sensitivity}

The default FAM architecture is the v24 canonical configuration ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads, $d_{ff} = 256$, $\approx$\,2.16M parameters). The legacy V17 variant ($d_{ff} = 1024$, 2.37M parameters, 790K predictor) is retained only inside the ablation tables of \cref{sec:ablations} for historical continuity. The main finding relevant to this paper is that the predictor's cross-horizon diversity matters far more than encoder depth or width: frozen\_multi's $F_w = 0.148$ (\cref{tab:finetune_ablation}) and the high inter-horizon cosine ($>0.98$) show that the horizon-conditioning signal in the pretrained predictor is under-used unless the predictor is finetuned. SIGReg-pred pretraining increases predictor diversity explicitly via spectral regularisation and improves downstream F1w by $+0.06$ (\cref{tab:sigreg_ablation}), consistent with this view. A full depth/width sweep is out of scope for this session; the architecture-decision log in \cref{app:decisions} records the choices with their evidence source.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Per-dataset pretraining, not cross-domain. A foundation model would pretrain on a union.
  \item Label efficiency varies by dataset: strong on C-MAPSS FD001, weaker on FD003/FD004.
  \item Anomaly ``prediction'' on SMAP is mostly within-segment continuation, not lead-time.
  \item SMD results are weak; we report them honestly.
  \item Pred-FT requires supervised training pairs $(x, y)$. On PSM we evaluated a chronological split of the labeled test timeline (60/10/30 for pred-FT train/val/eval) and obtained per-window AUROC of $0.55$ (probe\_h), $0.53$ (frozen\_multi), $0.46$ (pred\_ft) - all near chance, with individual seeds scattered from $0.47$ to $0.63$. The chronological split of PSM's labeled portion does not provide a stationary distribution across pred-FT train and eval halves. Unsupervised Mahalanobis scoring from the same pretrained encoder does not share this limitation (\cref{tab:benchmark}, PSM). This is an honest obstacle to a \emph{single} pipeline across all domains: pred-FT is the paper's preferred recipe when labels are available and stationary across the FT-train/eval split; Mahalanobis is the fallback when neither holds. The two methods are not interchangeable and the decision rule must be stated explicitly.
  \item \texttt{amazon/chronos-2} (multivariate, 120M encoder, frozen) matches FAM pred-FT on FD001 to within $0.0003$ AUPRC and is stronger at short horizons; FAM's advantage over Chronos-2 shows up on SMAP ($+0.110$ AUPRC) where the generic foundation-model pretraining corpus lacks coverage. A cross-domain FAM pretrained at comparable scale (not done here) is the natural next comparison.
  \item FD003 AUPRC drops from $0.932$ (v21 backbone, 790K predictor) to $0.766 \pm 0.009$ under the canonical v24 architecture (198K predictor, cumulative target interval). The multi-fault degradation mode appears to need more predictor capacity; we flag this as the one clear regression against v22.
  \item \textbf{Context length.} For prognostic datasets with a clear lifecycle start (C-MAPSS), feeding the full history from cycle 1 may improve failure prediction by exposing cumulative wear patterns invisible in a short sliding window. Our current experiments use full engine histories for C-MAPSS but sliding windows for streaming datasets. A controlled ablation of context length vs.\ prediction quality - especially on degradation datasets where early-life information may be predictive of late-life failure - is future work.
\end{itemize}

% 8. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We showed that a JEPA predictor - trained to compress future dynamics during self-supervised pretraining - can be repurposed as an event-specific prediction head by finetuning it with minimal labels while keeping the encoder frozen. This simple idea yields an $\approx$\,2.16M-parameter model that, at $10\%$ labels on C-MAPSS FD001, significantly beats a $3\times$-larger end-to-end baseline on AUPRC ($p=0.023$, $N{=}10$ matched seeds) and on 12 datasets across 8 domains reaches within 1 pp of a dedicated supervised ICU-mortality baseline (PhysioNet 2012 AUROC $0.858$ vs InceptionTime $0.868$) with no task-specific engineering. The multivariate foundation model \texttt{amazon/chronos-2} under an identical labeled-probe protocol ties or beats FAM on C-MAPSS (FD001 tie, FD002 $+0.008$, FD003 $+0.028$ for Chronos) and loses on spacecraft anomaly (SMAP) and cardiac (MBA) by $+0.11$ and $+0.03$ AUPRC; the per-dataset JEPA pretrain is therefore complementary, not superior. All tasks are evaluated through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ scored by AUPRC - threshold-free, robust to class imbalance, and enabling legacy metrics (RMSE, PA-F1, F1) to be recomputed from stored surfaces without rerunning inference. The encoder learns dynamics; the predictor learns the event.

\section{PA-F1 Inflation}
\label{app:pa_f1}

PA-F1 (Point-Adjusted F1) credits an entire anomaly segment as correctly predicted if any single timestep within it exceeds the threshold~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}. This protocol inflates reported F1 dramatically when anomaly segments are long and prevalence is non-trivial: the TSAD-Eval study~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval} shows that a random detector can exceed $F_1 = 0.9$ PA on some datasets. We concretely demonstrate this by contrasting PA-F1 and non-PA F1 for FAM+Mahalanobis on our benchmarks: SMAP $0.793$ PA vs.\ $0.038$ non-PA, SMD $0.252$ PA vs.\ $0.144$ non-PA, PSM $0.813$ PA vs.\ $0.085$ non-PA. The gap in PA-F1 between FAM and MTS-JEPA remains large even under a random-baseline sanity check (v18 phase 12 reports random-init + Mahalanobis at $0.604 \pm 0.007$ PA-F1 on SMAP; FAM is $0.79$), so the PA-F1 margin is not purely inflation - but any single PA-F1 number should be read in conjunction with the corresponding non-PA number.
