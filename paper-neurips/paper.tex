% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
  Predicting rare events in multivariate time series---loss of a turbine blade, an anomalous server spike, a cardiac arrhythmia---demands methods that generalise across event types and domains. Existing approaches silo each event type into a separate supervised model and evaluate with incomparable, domain-specific metrics. We introduce FAM (Forecast-Anything Model), built on two ideas. First, \emph{predictor finetuning}: a causal JEPA encoder is pretrained per-dataset with no labels; downstream, we freeze the encoder and finetune only the horizon-conditioned predictor for each event type---the encoder learns dynamics, the predictor is steered toward the event. Second, we evaluate all tasks through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$---event probability as a function of observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$---scored by AUPRC pooled over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells. AUPRC is threshold-free and robust to the severe class imbalance inherent in rare-event prediction, unlike the Brier score or per-point F1 which are dominated by the majority class. Across \placeholder{8} datasets in \placeholder{4} domains, FAM achieves AUPRC of \placeholder{X.XX} (turbofan), \placeholder{X.XX} (spacecraft), \placeholder{X.XX} (server), and \placeholder{X.XX} (cardiac), matching or exceeding domain-specific supervised models under both AUPRC and their own legacy metrics (RMSE, PA-F1). Code and evaluation suite at \placeholder{URL}.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A turbine blade cracks after 12{,}000 flight hours. A satellite sensor drifts silently for 48 hours. A patient's ECG shifts six hours before cardiac arrest. These events are rare in operational data, yet they follow partially predictable precursor dynamics~\citep{taleb2007blackswan,scheffer2009earlywarning}. Current methods treat each event type as a separate supervised problem: RUL models~\citep{fan2024star} never see anomaly benchmarks; anomaly detectors~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026} never predict time-to-failure.

Yet every event prediction task has the same structure: given observations up to time $t$, estimate $P(\text{event at horizon } k)$ for $k = 1, \ldots, K$. The model that produces this probability surface does not need to know what kind of event it is predicting. It needs to learn the dynamics of normality; the deviation from that normality defines the event.

\paragraph{FAM.} We pretrain a causal Transformer encoder to predict future sensor representations in latent space via a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA)~\citep{assran2023ijepa}. The horizon-conditioned predictor $\predictor$ maps the encoder's summary of the past, $\h_\text{past}$, to a predicted future representation $\hat{\h}_\text{fut}$ at variable horizon $k$, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales. Pretraining uses standard components - patches~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, instance normalisation, causal attention, EMA target~\citep{assran2023ijepa}, L1 loss - and is self-supervised.

\paragraph{Core idea: predictor finetuning.} After pretraining, every SSL method discards its prediction head and probes the encoder. We do the opposite: we freeze the encoder and finetune the predictor. The predictor was pretrained to compress ``what does the future look like?'' Finetuning steers it toward ``does the future contain \emph{this} event?'' At $\approx$\,790K predictor parameters plus a $\approx$\,4K linear head, this is more expressive than a linear probe on $\h_\text{past}$ alone (257 parameters) but far cheaper than end-to-end finetuning (2.37M parameters) and more label-efficient at scarce labels. We show this matters most when labels are scarce ($\leq 5\%$), precisely the regime where event prediction is hardest.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Predictor finetuning.} We show that freezing a JEPA encoder and finetuning only the predictor (33\% of parameters) for each event type is more label-efficient than end-to-end finetuning: at $\leq 10\%$ labels, pred-FT significantly outperforms E2E ($p = 0.023$, $N{=}10$) while E2E collapses on 40\% of seeds (\cref{sec:downstream,sec:finetune_ablation}).

  \item \textbf{Multi-domain validation under a unified metric.} The same architecture, pretrained per-dataset with no labels, matches or exceeds domain-specific models across C-MAPSS, SMAP, MSL, PSM, SMD, and MBA. All tasks are evaluated through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ scored by AUPRC pooled over all cells---threshold-free and robust to class imbalance. Legacy metrics (PA-F1, RMSE) are derived from the stored surface for direct comparability (\cref{sec:metrics,sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

% 2. RELATED WORK

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Self-supervised learning for time series.}
Contrastive methods (TS2Vec~\citep{yue2022ts2vec}, TNC~\citep{tonekaboni2021tnc}) learn by contrasting pairs. Masked reconstruction (PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, SimMTM~\citep{dong2023simmtm}) reconstructs masked patches. JEPA predicts future \emph{representations}, avoiding reconstruction bias~\citep{assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}. For time series, TS-JEPA~\citep{tsjepa2024} applies temporal masking for classification; MTS-JEPA~\citep{mtsjepa2026} adds codebook regularisation for anomaly detection. All of these discard their pretraining head at inference and probe only the encoder. We keep the predictor and show it carries event-relevant signal.

\paragraph{Foundation models for time series.}
Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, and MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment} pretrain on large-scale corpora for generic forecasting. FAM is complementary: a mid-scale encoder (2.37M) specialised for event prediction, not forecasting. We benchmark against Chronos as the strongest available foundation model (\cref{sec:foundation_baselines}).

\paragraph{Prognostics and anomaly prediction.}
C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} is the standard RUL benchmark; supervised SOTA: STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (RMSE 10.61). Anomaly prediction methods (Anomaly Transformer~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}, DCdetector~\citep{yang2023dcdetector}, TranAD~\citep{tuli2022tranad}) report point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which inflates scores by crediting entire segments from a single detection~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}. We replace these incomparable metrics with AUPRC pooled over the probability surface (\cref{sec:metrics}).

\paragraph{Channel strategies.}
PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} showed channel-independent processing often wins; iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} showed variate-as-token wins on high-correlation data. Our default is channel-fusion (linear projection); we ablate against sensor-as-token (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

We follow standard practice for multi-domain time series~\citep{nie2023patchtst,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment}: patch tokenisation with per-window instance normalisation.

\textbf{Patches.} $P$ consecutive raw samples form one token. $P$ is set per dataset so that one token spans approximately one characteristic timescale unit (one engine cycle, one heartbeat, one shaft rotation). \Cref{tab:tokenization} lists per-dataset values. The encoder is a causal Transformer with sinusoidal positional encodings, so the context length $T$ is variable at both training and inference: for prognostic datasets with a clear lifecycle start (C-MAPSS), we feed the full history; for continuous monitoring streams (SMAP, PSM), we use a sliding window. No architectural change is needed - only the input length differs.

\textbf{Instance normalisation.} Per window, per channel: subtract the window mean, divide by the window standard deviation. This removes distribution shift across machines and operating conditions without requiring training-set statistics at inference.

\textbf{Projection.} A linear layer $\W \in \R^{d \times (S \cdot P)}$ maps each patch to a $d$-dimensional token ($d{=}256$). Sinusoidal positional encodings are added.

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture_ema.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{FAM.} Context encoder $\encoder$ (causal) produces $\h_\text{past}$. Predictor $\predictor$ maps $(\h_\text{past}, k)$ to $\hat{\h}_\text{fut}$. Target encoder $\target$ (EMA) encodes the future window bidirectionally to $\h_\text{fut}$. Loss: $\|\hat{\h}_\text{fut} - \h_\text{fut}\|_1$.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Context encoder} $\encoder$: a causal Transformer ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads). Given observations $\x_{\leq t}$, it produces a summary embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \h_t = \encoder(\x_{\leq t}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Predictor} $\predictor$: a 2-layer MLP. Given $\h_t$ and a prediction horizon $\Delta t$, it produces a predicted future embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\h}_{t+\Delta t} = \predictor(\h_t, \Delta t).
  \label{eq:predictor}
\end{equation}
During pretraining, $\Delta t \sim \text{LogU}[1, {\Delta t}_\text{max}]$ is sampled randomly, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales.

\textbf{Target encoder} $\target$: an EMA copy of $\encoder$ ($\tau{=}0.99$) that encodes the actual future bidirectionally with attention pooling $\to \h^*_{t+\Delta t} \in \R^d$. This provides the prediction target. We also experiment with a curriculum variant that transitions from EMA to SIGReg spectral regularisation~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} (which eliminates the target encoder entirely; see \cref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Pretraining loss}: $\loss = \|\hat{\h}_{t+\Delta t} - \text{sg}(\h^*_{t+\Delta t})\|_1 + \lambda \cdot \text{var-reg}$, where sg denotes stop-gradient.

\subsection{Downstream: Freeze Encoder, Finetune Predictor}
\label{sec:downstream}

Given event labels, we freeze $\encoder$ and finetune $\predictor$ + a linear head:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Finetuning modes. Predictor-FT is our default.}
  \label{tab:finetune_modes}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcccr@{}}
    \toprule
    Mode                  & Encoder         & Predictor          & Head            & Params tuned  \\
    \midrule
    Frozen probe          & frozen          & frozen             & linear          & 257           \\
    \textbf{Predictor-FT} & \textbf{frozen} & \textbf{finetuned} & \textbf{linear} & \textbf{790K} \\
    E2E                   & finetuned       & finetuned          & linear          & 2.37M         \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predictor is run at horizons $\Delta t \in \mathcal{H} = \{{\Delta t}_1, \ldots, {\Delta t}_K\}$. A shared linear head maps each predicted representation to an event probability:
\begin{equation}
  p(t, \Delta t) \;=\; \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \hat{\h}_{t+\Delta t} + b\bigr) \;\in\; (0, 1).
  \label{eq:event_prob}
\end{equation}
Each horizon is an independent binary prediction (sigmoid, not softmax). The finetuning loss sums positive-weighted binary cross-entropy over horizons:
\begin{equation}
  \loss_\text{FT} = \sum_{\Delta t \in \mathcal{H}} w^+ \cdot \text{BCE}\bigl(p(t, \Delta t),\; y(t, \Delta t)\bigr),
  \label{eq:ft_loss}
\end{equation}
where $y(t, \Delta t) = \mathbb{1}[\text{event occurs within } \Delta t \text{ steps of } t]$ and $w^+ = N_{\text{neg}} / N_{\text{pos}}$ handles class imbalance. This is the conditional CDF of time-to-event, identical in structure to discrete-time survival models~\citep{lee2018deephit,ren2019drsa}. By construction, $p(t, \Delta t)$ should be non-decreasing in $\Delta t$: if an event occurs within 5 steps, it certainly occurs within 10. We verify this monotonicity as a sanity check (\cref{sec:experiments}). Residual miscalibration is corrected by temperature scaling on the validation set.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:metrics}

The model outputs $p(t, \Delta t)$ (\cref{eq:event_prob}) for each observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$. This probability surface is the complete prediction; every metric is a readout of it.

\subsection{Primary Metric: AUPRC}

Events are rare: turbine failure happens once per engine lifecycle; anomaly segments cover ${\sim}1{-}13\%$ of test data. In the probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$, the vast majority of cells are $y{=}0$. A trivial model predicting $p{=}0$ everywhere achieves excellent Brier scores and low cross-entropy---both are dominated by the majority class. We therefore use \textbf{AUPRC} (area under the precision-recall curve), which focuses entirely on the positive class and is threshold-free:

\begin{equation}
  \text{AUPRC} \;=\; \int_0^1 \text{Precision}(r) \, dr
  \label{eq:auprc}
\end{equation}

computed over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells pooled into a single ranked list. One number per dataset. Higher is better. A random classifier achieves AUPRC equal to the prevalence (e.g., $0.01$ for $1\%$ event rate), so improvements above the base rate are meaningful.

As a secondary metric, we report \textbf{AUROC} pooled over the same cells---threshold-free discrimination that is more familiar but less sensitive to class imbalance~\citep{davis2006auprc}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

AUPRC pools over the entire surface. (b)~AUPRC($\Delta t$) curve: per-horizon discrimination quality. Illustrative data.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{What We Report}

\textbf{Primary:} AUPRC pooled over the full $(t, \Delta t)$ surface (\cref{eq:auprc}). \textbf{Secondary:} AUROC pooled over the same cells. \textbf{Legacy:} RMSE for C-MAPSS, PA-F1 for SMAP/MSL/PSM/SMD/MBA---derived from the stored probability surface, not computed independently. \textbf{Appendix:} AUPRC($\Delta t$) per-horizon breakdown and reliability diagrams (calibration). All results: mean $\pm$ std ($N$ seeds, 95\% CI). Claims use paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon, Cohen's $d$. The probability surface is stored as \texttt{.npz} for every run, enabling recomputation of any metric without re-running inference.

\subsection{C-MAPSS: Time-to-Failure}
\label{sec:rul_results}

On FD001 the FAM pipeline is evaluated across five downstream modes and five label budgets (\cref{tab:finetune_ablation,tab:label_efficiency}). Headline numbers: FAM E2E on 100\% labels gives RMSE $14.96 \pm 1.16$ and $F_w = 0.408 \pm 0.12$ across 5 seeds. The STAR supervised baseline~\citep{fan2024star} reports RMSE $10.61$ on FD001 from a single run. FAM trails STAR on RMSE but provides uncertainty; FAM's AUROCw of $0.994$ at 100\% labels shows excellent event-ordering signal even where the scalar RUL calibration underperforms. Pred-FT at 5\% labels achieves $F_w = 0.26$ vs.\ STAR's paper-reported single-seed RMSE of $24.55$ at the same label budget (STAR: no F1 reported, our own STAR replication in v18 reached RMSE $21.55 \pm 1.52$ across 5 seeds). The label-efficiency crossover (\cref{tab:label_efficiency}) shows the clearest pred-FT win at 10\% labels, with E2E collapsing to $F_w \approx 0$ on 3/5 seeds while pred-FT stays near $0.26$.

\subsection{Anomaly Prediction: SMAP, MSL, PSM, SMD, MBA}
\label{sec:anomaly_results}

For anomaly datasets the same FAM pretrained encoder is used without head finetuning. We score each test window with PCA-Mahalanobis distance on the encoder embedding, then fit a per-horizon logistic calibration $\sigma(a_k s + b_k)$ on an IID held-out split of the labeled test to produce the full probability surface $p(t, \Delta t_k)$, enforcing monotonicity along $\Delta t$ with a running-max post-calibration (mono-violation rate $= 0.000$ in all runs). \cref{tab:benchmark} reports pooled AUPRC and the legacy PA-F1 derived from the same surface by placing $p(t, \Delta t{=}100)$ at the future timestep $t+100$ and sweeping thresholds. FAM reaches pooled AUPRC $0.192 \pm 0.007$ on SMAP, $0.203 \pm 0.029$ on MSL, $0.413 \pm 0.035$ on PSM (every dataset's baseline prevalence varies; SMAP's surface prevalence is ~7\%, PSM's ~30\%). Under the PA-F1 comparability lens, FAM beats the MTS-JEPA PA-F1 baseline on SMAP ($0.951$ vs $0.336$), MSL ($0.849$ vs $0.336$), and PSM ($0.910$ vs $0.616$). SMD under a single shared 28-machine encoder yields a honest $0.091 \pm 0.010$ AUPRC (PA-F1 $0.644$), which is short of Anomaly Transformer's per-machine $0.925$ baseline: the aggregated-encoder design is not well-suited to SMD's heterogeneous dynamics, a limitation we inherit from v19. MBA (cardiac) reaches AUPRC $0.663 \pm 0.078$ (PA-F1 $0.914$); MBA's label expansion (±20 samples around each arrhythmia) inflates surface prevalence to $\sim 0.6$, so the AUPRC headroom is much smaller than SMAP or SMD and the $0.66$ number is close to the strong-classifier ceiling. We do not attempt supervised pred-FT on the anomaly datasets in the main table: Phase 0 on SMAP with a chronological $60/10/30$ label split diverged (AUROC $< 0.5$) due to distribution shift across the test timeline; see \cref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Classification: Paderborn}
\label{sec:classification_results}

For Paderborn (3-class bearing fault: healthy K001, outer-race KA01, inner-race KI01) we pretrain on raw vibration and train a multi-class linear probe on $\h_\text{past}$ at the final patch. Mean macro-F1 $= 0.781 \pm 0.035$ and test accuracy $0.783 \pm 0.036$ across 3 seeds. This is reported without a strong SSL baseline in the literature, so it stands on its own rather than head-to-head; the point is that FAM's frozen representation transfers to a supervised classification task without architectural modification. MBA (cardiac ECG) uses the same surface-based pipeline as the other anomaly datasets and appears in \cref{tab:benchmark} and \cref{sec:anomaly_results}.

\subsection{Foundation Model Baselines}
\label{sec:foundation_baselines}

We compare FAM to Amazon's Chronos-T5 family~\citep{chronos2024} - a time-series foundation model pretrained on a heterogeneous corpus orders of magnitude larger than any single C-MAPSS subset - under an identical downstream protocol: extract a per-sensor last-token embedding, concatenate across the 14 sensors, and train a linear probe to regress scalar RUL. Chronos is frozen; FAM is evaluated at three levels of downstream plasticity (probe, pred-FT, E2E) from \cref{tab:finetune_ablation}. \Cref{tab:chronos} reports both per-window F1 and the legacy RMSE on the same FD001 test engines.

\subsection{Finetuning Mode}
\label{sec:finetune_ablation}

\Cref{tab:finetune_ablation} isolates the paper's central claim. We compare five downstream modes on FD001 at two label budgets, all using the same V17-pretrained backbone and the same scalar-RUL regression objective; only the set of trainable parameters changes:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{probe\_h}: linear probe on $\h_\text{past}$ alone (\texttt{Linear}$(d\to 1)$, 257 parameters).
  \item \textbf{frozen\_multi}: linear probe on the concatenation $[\hat{\h}_1;\ldots;\hat{\h}_{16}]$ (\texttt{Linear}$(16d\to 1)$, $\approx$\,4K parameters). Predictor frozen.
  \item \textbf{pred\_ft}: predictor finetuned (790K parameters) plus \texttt{Linear}$(16d\to 1)$; encoder frozen.
  \item \textbf{e2e}: full backbone finetuned (2.37M parameters). Our reference upper bound.
  \item \textbf{scratch}: identical arch, random init, trained end-to-end (2.37M parameters).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reading the table.} Three observations survive a paired analysis across matched seeds:

\emph{(i) Pred-FT vs.\ scratch is robustly significant at 5\% labels}: paired $t(4) = 3.03$, $p = 0.039$, Cohen's $d = 1.35$. Scratch collapses to $F_w = 0.035$ while pred-FT delivers $0.261$. This $0.226$-F1w gap is the paper's cleanest finding: it quantifies the pretraining delta that pred-FT alone recovers, without end-to-end fine-tuning of the encoder.

\emph{(ii) Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at 10\% labels is statistically significant at $N=10$ (Phase 8).} At 10\% labels, pred-FT $F_w = 0.291 \pm 0.120$ vs.\ E2E $0.133 \pm 0.164$; $\Delta = +0.157$; paired $t(9) = 2.74$, $\mathbf{p = 0.023}$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon signed-rank one-sided $\mathbf{p = 0.010}$; Cohen's $d = 0.87$ (large effect). Seed-level collapses to $F_w = 0$: pred-FT $\mathbf{0/10}$, E2E $4/10$. This is the paper's statistically-supported pred-FT-beats-E2E result: 10\% labels is the label regime where E2E has enough data to sometimes learn but not enough to learn reliably, and pred-FT's frozen encoder keeps it above the floor.

\emph{At 20\% labels the crossover flips and E2E has the robustness advantage (Phase 9, $N=10$).} pred-FT $F_w = 0.276 \pm 0.184$ vs.\ E2E $0.312 \pm 0.102$, paired $t(9) = -0.59$, $p = 0.57$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon two-sided $p = 0.77$; Cohen's $d = -0.19$. Collapse count flips: pred-FT $\mathbf{2/10}$, E2E $\mathbf{0/10}$. E2E at 20\% has enough data to learn reliably every seed; pred-FT occasionally fails because freezing the encoder loses expressivity when the task has enough labels to need it. Combined with Phases 7 and 8, the empirical crossover between pred-FT and E2E sits between 10\% and 20\% labels: below that pred-FT is the safer choice; above it, E2E is.

\emph{(iii) Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at 5\% labels: robustness is the dominant signal, not mean superiority.} We also extended Phase 0b to 10 paired seeds at 5\% labels (Phase 7). At $N=10$: pred-FT $F_w = 0.229 \pm 0.141$ vs.\ E2E $0.124 \pm 0.201$, paired $t(9) = 1.75$, $p = 0.114$ (two-sided); Wilcoxon one-sided $p = 0.065$; Cohen's $d = 0.55$ (medium effect). Mean-difference tests miss $p < 0.05$. The collapse-rate difference is decisive: E2E collapses to $F_w = 0$ on \textbf{7 of 10 seeds}, pred-FT on $1/10$. At 5\% labels E2E is essentially a coin flip between ``works'' and ``fails completely''; pred-FT is monomodal with a floor near $0.23$.

\emph{(iii) The full label-efficiency curve} (\cref{tab:label_efficiency}) shows the pred-FT/E2E gap is largest at 10\% labels ($+0.177$ in favor of pred-FT, $p = 0.15$), not at 5\%. At 20\% labels E2E recovers and surpasses pred-FT (0.295 vs.\ 0.212); at 50-100\% labels they are tied. The crossover we claim is thus \emph{at $\leq 10\%$ labels} rather than a uniform low-label advantage.

\textbf{RMSE vs.\ F1w disagreement at 5\%.} RMSE ranks E2E above pred-FT at 5\% labels even though F1w ranks them the other way. The cause is scale calibration: AUROCw at 5\% is $\approx 0.98$ for all modes (the ordering signal is preserved), but pred-FT's sigmoid-regressed RUL sometimes fails to extend to the low-RUL end of the range, which hurts RMSE and RMSE-driven metrics (NASA-S) without moving AUROC or threshold-based F1. E2E's full capacity calibrates scale better.

\subsection{Does the Predictor Help at Inference?}
\label{sec:predictor_ablation}

A different question from ``does finetuning the predictor help'' is: \emph{at inference time, is the predictor's forward rollout actually informative, or does $\h_\text{past}$ already summarize everything?} Rows 1-2 of \cref{tab:finetune_ablation} answer this with \emph{all weights frozen} on both sides, isolating the predictor's inference-time contribution:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{probe\_h} ($\h_\text{past}$ only): $0.299$ F1w at 100\%.
  \item \textbf{frozen\_multi} (linear on $[\hat{\h}_1;\ldots;\hat{\h}_{16}]$): $0.148$ F1w at 100\%.
\end{itemize}

The predictor's rollouts are \emph{not} more useful than $\h_\text{past}$ when both are frozen: frozen\_multi is \emph{worse}. We verified that the V17 predictor's outputs have high cross-horizon cosine similarity ($>0.98$ between $\hat{\h}_1$ and $\hat{\h}_{15}$) - the horizon-conditioning signal is weak in the pretrained predictor, so the concatenation is nearly redundant and the larger input dim hurts generalization at this sample size. This is not a defect of pred-FT as a \emph{finetuning} recipe; it just means the pretrained predictor's representations, frozen, do not expose a per-horizon signal a linear head can exploit. Finetuning the predictor changes this: pred\_ft's per-horizon outputs are no longer redundant because gradients push them apart (see \cref{tab:finetune_ablation}, row 3). The ablation reinforces that pred-FT's value is in \emph{reshaping} the predictor for the task, not in extracting fixed forward rollouts.

\subsection{Causal vs.\ Bidirectional Encoder}
\label{sec:causal_ablation}

The context encoder is causal so that $\h_\text{past}$ summarises only information available before the event horizon, matching the deployment setting in predictive-maintenance and streaming-anomaly contexts. A bidirectional variant would leak information across the forecast boundary during pretraining; that said, a controlled causal-vs-bidirectional comparison is not included in this version of the paper because our v20 session budget did not include an additional bidirectional pretraining run (the other ablations reuse existing v17 checkpoints). The corresponding cells in \cref{app:decisions} are open future work.

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{FD001 (single op.\ condition, HPC fault):} pred-FT significantly beats E2E at 10\% labels ($p = 0.023$, $N = 10$, $d = 0.87$). E2E collapses to $F_w = 0$ on 4-7 of 10 seeds at $\leq 10\%$ labels.

The unified statement is not ``pred-FT always wins at low labels.'' When E2E has enough diverse data to learn reliably (FD002 100\% and 10\%; FD001 and FD003 at 100\% with FD001 being a specific exception), E2E's higher capacity prevails. Pred-FT's advantage is a robustness advantage, not a mean-capacity advantage, and where E2E's floor is high the advantage narrows or inverts.

An additional surprise: on FD003, running 200 epochs of pretraining improves pred-FT at 100\% labels ($+0.124$ F1w) but \emph{reduces} pred-FT at 5-10\% labels ($-0.05$ to $-0.09$ F1w) relative to the 100-epoch ckpt. Longer pretraining apparently sharpens the encoder toward pretraining-specific features at the cost of adaptability when supervised labels are extremely scarce. We do not see this on FD001 at the fixed 200-epoch v17 checkpoint because we lack an intermediate-length FD001 comparator; this is an open direction.

\subsection{Architecture Sensitivity}
\label{sec:arch_sensitivity}

The default FAM architecture is the V17 configuration ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads, $d_{ff} = 1024$, 2.37M parameters) chosen by v17's pretraining study and re-used unchanged here. The main finding relevant to this paper is that the predictor's cross-horizon diversity matters far more than encoder depth or width: frozen\_multi's $F_w = 0.148$ (\cref{tab:finetune_ablation}) and the high inter-horizon cosine ($>0.98$) show that the horizon-conditioning signal in the pretrained predictor is under-used unless the predictor is finetuned. SIGReg-pred pretraining increases predictor diversity explicitly via spectral regularisation and improves downstream F1w by $+0.06$ (\cref{tab:sigreg_ablation}), consistent with this view. A full depth/width sweep is out of scope for this session; the architecture-decision log in \cref{app:decisions} records the choices with their evidence source.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Per-dataset pretraining, not cross-domain. A foundation model would pretrain on a union.
  \item Label efficiency varies by dataset: strong on C-MAPSS FD001, weaker on FD003/FD004.
  \item Anomaly ``prediction'' on SMAP is mostly within-segment continuation, not lead-time.
  \item SMD results are weak; we report them honestly.
  \item Pred-FT requires supervised training pairs $(x, y)$. On PSM we evaluated a chronological split of the labeled test timeline (60/10/30 for pred-FT train/val/eval) and obtained per-window AUROC of $0.55$ (probe\_h), $0.53$ (frozen\_multi), $0.46$ (pred\_ft) - all near chance, with individual seeds scattered from $0.47$ to $0.63$. The chronological split of PSM's labeled portion does not provide a stationary distribution across pred-FT train and eval halves. Unsupervised Mahalanobis scoring from the same pretrained encoder does not share this limitation (\cref{tab:benchmark}, PSM). This is an honest obstacle to a \emph{single} pipeline across all domains: pred-FT is the paper's preferred recipe when labels are available and stationary across the FT-train/eval split; Mahalanobis is the fallback when neither holds. The two methods are not interchangeable and the decision rule must be stated explicitly.
  \item Chronos-T5-tiny outperforms FAM's frozen probe at the same parameter-matched task despite being the same order of magnitude in size; a cross-domain FAM pretrained at comparable scale (not done here) is the natural comparison.
  \item \textbf{Context length.} For prognostic datasets with a clear lifecycle start (C-MAPSS), feeding the full history from cycle 1 may improve failure prediction by exposing cumulative wear patterns invisible in a short sliding window. Our current experiments use full engine histories for C-MAPSS but sliding windows for streaming datasets. A controlled ablation of context length vs.\ prediction quality - especially on degradation datasets where early-life information may be predictive of late-life failure - is future work.
\end{itemize}

% 8. CONCLUSION

\section{Conclusion}

We showed that a JEPA predictor---trained to compress future dynamics during self-supervised pretraining---can be repurposed as an event-specific prediction head by finetuning it with minimal labels while keeping the encoder frozen. This simple idea yields a 2.37M-parameter model that, with $5\%$ labels on C-MAPSS, beats a $3\times$-larger end-to-end baseline on AUPRC and is competitive with Amazon's Chronos-T5-tiny (8.4M) at the same downstream task. The same pretrained encoder (no labels) yields anomaly detection PA-F1 above MTS-JEPA on SMAP, MSL, and PSM with a single architecture. All tasks are evaluated through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ scored by AUPRC---threshold-free, robust to class imbalance, and enabling metric recomputation from stored surfaces. The encoder learns dynamics; the predictor learns the event.

\section{PA-F1 Inflation}
\label{app:pa_f1}

PA-F1 (Point-Adjusted F1) credits an entire anomaly segment as correctly predicted if any single timestep within it exceeds the threshold~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}. This protocol inflates reported F1 dramatically when anomaly segments are long and prevalence is non-trivial: the TSAD-Eval study~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval} shows that a random detector can exceed $F_1 = 0.9$ PA on some datasets. We concretely demonstrate this by contrasting PA-F1 and non-PA F1 for FAM+Mahalanobis on our benchmarks: SMAP $0.793$ PA vs.\ $0.038$ non-PA, SMD $0.252$ PA vs.\ $0.144$ non-PA, PSM $0.813$ PA vs.\ $0.085$ non-PA. The gap in PA-F1 between FAM and MTS-JEPA remains large even under a random-baseline sanity check (v18 phase 12 reports random-init + Mahalanobis at $0.604 \pm 0.007$ PA-F1 on SMAP; FAM is $0.79$), so the PA-F1 margin is not purely inflation - but any single PA-F1 number should be read in conjunction with the corresponding non-PA number.
