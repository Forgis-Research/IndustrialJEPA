% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
  Predicting events in multivariate time series (loss of a turbine blade, an anomalous server spike, a cardiac arrhythmia) demands methods that generalise across event types and domains, especially when labels are scarce. Existing approaches silo each event type into a separate supervised model and evaluate with incomparable, domain-specific metrics. We introduce FAM (Forecast-Anything Model), built on two ideas. First, \emph{predictor finetuning}: a causal encoder pretrained via a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) learns to predict future representations in latent space with no labels; downstream, we freeze the encoder and finetune only the horizon-conditioned predictor (${\sim}9\%$ of parameters) for each event type. Second, we evaluate all tasks through a probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ - event probability as a function of observation time and prediction horizon $\Delta t$ - parameterized as a discrete-time survival CDF that is monotonically non-decreasing by construction. Across 11 datasets in 7 domains, a single 2.16M-parameter architecture with no dataset-specific hyperparameters outperforms Chronos-2 (a 120M-parameter foundation model) on 7 of 8 comparable datasets when using identical downstream heads (198K trainable parameters each), demonstrating that domain-specific JEPA pretraining yields stronger event-prediction representations than value-forecasting pretraining at 56$\times$ lower total model cost. At 10\% of labels, FAM retains ${\geq}98\%$ of its full-label performance on lifecycle datasets; at 2\% (just 2 engines out of 85), it retains $92\%$. Code and stored probability surfaces at \url{https://github.com/Forgis-Research/IndustrialJEPA}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A turbine blade cracks after 12{,}000 flight hours. A satellite sensor drifts silently for 48 hours. A patient's ECG shifts six hours before cardiac arrest. These events are rare in operational data, yet they follow partially predictable precursor dynamics~\citep{taleb2007blackswan,scheffer2009earlywarning}. Current methods treat each event type as a separate supervised problem: RUL models~\citep{fan2024star} never see anomaly benchmarks; anomaly detectors~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026} never predict time-to-failure.

Yet every event prediction task has the same structure: given observations up to time $t$, estimate $P(\text{event within } \Delta t)$ for each horizon $\Delta t$. The \emph{encoder} that learns these dynamics does not need to know which event will matter downstream; it learns general temporal structure from unlabeled data. The \emph{predictor}, finetuned with a small number of event labels, specialises the learned dynamics to the specific event of interest: a mechanical failure, a cardiac arrhythmia, or a cyber-attack.

\paragraph{FAM.} The key design choice is \emph{what} to predict. Forecasting models, including foundation models like Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos} and TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, optimise for reconstructing future values, so their representations must retain every source of variation that affects the output: sensor noise, irrelevant oscillations, stochastic perturbations. JEPA~\citep{assran2023ijepa} predicts future \emph{representations} with no reconstruction obligation: the encoder is free to learn a latent space that retains what is predictable about the future and discards what is not. Unpredictable noise is compressed away by the representation bottleneck; predictable precursor dynamics, the signal that event prediction depends on, are preserved because the pretraining loss rewards their retention (\cref{sec:theory}). We apply this to time series: a causal Transformer encodes observations up to $t$ into $\h_t$; a horizon-conditioned predictor $\predictor$ maps $(\h_t, \Delta t)$ to a predicted future representation, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales. Pretraining uses standard components (patches~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, instance normalisation~\citep{kim2022revin}, causal attention, EMA target) and is self-supervised.

\paragraph{Core idea: predictor finetuning.} After pretraining, every SSL method discards its prediction head and probes the encoder. We do the opposite: freeze the encoder, finetune the predictor. The predictor is a small horizon-conditioned MLP (${\sim}9\%$ of parameters) that, together with a per-horizon event head, is more expressive than a linear probe but far cheaper than end-to-end finetuning. The output is parameterized as a discrete-time survival CDF~\citep{lee2018deephit}, guaranteeing that event probabilities are monotonically non-decreasing in the prediction horizon by construction.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Predictor finetuning as the downstream recipe.} Freeze the encoder, finetune the predictor + head. This achieves the same AUPRC as end-to-end finetuning with ${\sim}11{\times}$ fewer trainable parameters (198K vs.\ 2.16M) and tighter cross-seed variance at low label budgets (\cref{sec:finetune_ablation,sec:label_efficiency}).

  \item \textbf{Multi-domain event prediction under a unified metric.} One architecture across 11 datasets in 7 domains, evaluated through the same probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ scored by AUPRC, threshold-free and robust to class imbalance (\cref{sec:metrics,sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Self-supervised learning for time series.}
Contrastive methods (TS2Vec~\citep{yue2022ts2vec}, TNC~\citep{tonekaboni2021tnc}) learn by contrasting pairs. Masked reconstruction (PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, SimMTM~\citep{dong2023simmtm}) reconstructs masked patches. JEPA predicts future \emph{representations}, avoiding reconstruction bias~\citep{assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}. For time series, TS-JEPA~\citep{tsjepa2024} applies temporal masking for classification; MTS-JEPA~\citep{mtsjepa2026} adds codebook regularisation for anomaly detection. All of these discard their pretraining head at inference and probe only the encoder. We keep the predictor and show it carries event-relevant signal.

\paragraph{Foundation models for time series.}
Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, and MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment} pretrain on large-scale corpora for generic value forecasting (predicting next values). FAM targets a different output: event probabilities rather than future channel values. The encoder is mid-scale ($\approx$\,2.16M) and pretrained per-dataset rather than on a massive corpus; what transfers across domains is the \emph{recipe} (architecture + predictor finetuning), not the weights. We benchmark against \texttt{amazon/chronos-2}~(\cref{app:chronos_full}).

\paragraph{Prognostics and anomaly prediction.}
C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} is the standard RUL benchmark; supervised SOTA: STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (RMSE 10.61). Anomaly prediction methods (Anomaly Transformer~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}, DCdetector~\citep{yang2023dcdetector}, TranAD~\citep{tuli2022tranad}) report point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which inflates scores by crediting entire segments from a single detection~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}. We replace these incomparable metrics with AUPRC pooled over the probability surface (\cref{sec:metrics}).

\paragraph{Channel strategies.}
PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} showed channel-independent processing often wins; iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} showed variate-as-token wins on high-correlation data. Our default is channel-fusion (linear projection); we ablate against sensor-as-token (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

We follow standard practice for multi-domain time series~\citep{nie2023patchtst,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment}: patch tokenisation with per-context instance normalisation.

\textbf{Patches.} $P{=}16$ consecutive raw samples form one token. This is fixed for all datasets with sufficient temporal resolution (sample rate $\geq$ 1/min); for coarsely sampled data (PhysioNet at 1/hour), we use $P{=}1$ so that the causal transformer receives enough tokens. \Cref{tab:tokenization} lists the resulting token counts. The encoder requires a minimum context of ${\sim}128$ timesteps (${\sim}8$ tokens) for self-attention to be effective. The context length is variable at both training and inference: for prognostic datasets with a clear lifecycle start (C-MAPSS), we feed the full history; for continuous monitoring streams (SMAP, PSM), we use a sliding window of 512 steps.

\textbf{Instance normalisation.} Per context, per channel: subtract the context mean, divide by the context standard deviation. This removes distribution shift across machines and operating conditions without requiring training-set statistics at inference.

\textbf{Projection.} A linear layer $\W \in \R^{d \times (C \cdot P)}$ maps each patch to a $d$-dimensional token ($d{=}256$; $C$ = number of channels, $P$ = patch length). Sinusoidal positional encodings are added.

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{FAM architecture.} \emph{Top:} pretraining. The context encoder $\encoder$ (causal) produces $\h_t$; the predictor $\predictor$ maps $(\h_t, \Delta t)$ to $\hat{\h}$; the EMA target encoder $\target$ (bidirectional + attention pool) provides the L1 target $\h^*$ from the interval $\x_{(t, t+\Delta t]}$. \emph{Bottom:} finetuning. The encoder is frozen; the predictor runs at $K$ horizons; per-horizon hazard logits pass through sigmoid $\to$ conditional hazards $\lambda_k$ $\to$ cumulative product $\to$ survival CDF $p(t, \Delta t_k)$, which is monotonically non-decreasing by construction.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Context encoder} $\encoder$: a causal Transformer ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads). Given observations $\x_{\leq t}$, it produces a summary embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \h_t = \encoder(\x_{\leq t}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Predictor} $\predictor$: a 2-layer MLP. Given $\h_t$ and a prediction horizon $\Delta t$, it produces a predicted embedding of the future interval $(t, t{+}\Delta t]$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} = \predictor(\h_t, \Delta t).
  \label{eq:predictor}
\end{equation}
During pretraining, $\Delta t \sim \text{LogU}[1, {\Delta t}_\text{max}]$ is sampled randomly, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales.

\textbf{Target encoder} $\target$: an EMA copy of $\encoder$ ($\tau{=}0.99$) that encodes the next $\Delta t$ steps $\x_{(t, t+\Delta t]}$ bidirectionally with attention pooling $\to \h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]} \in \R^d$. Crucially, the target interval starts immediately at $t$; there is no gap between context and target. The same interval is used for event prediction downstream (\cref{sec:downstream}). We also experiment with a curriculum variant that transitions from EMA to SIGReg spectral regularisation~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} (which eliminates the target encoder entirely; see \cref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Pretraining loss}: $\loss = \|\hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} - \text{sg}(\h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]})\|_1 + \lambda \cdot \text{var-reg}$, where sg denotes stop-gradient.

\subsection{Downstream: Freeze Encoder, Finetune Predictor}
\label{sec:downstream}

Given event labels, we freeze $\encoder$ and finetune $\predictor$ + a linear head:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Finetuning modes. Predictor-FT is our default.}
  \label{tab:finetune_modes}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcccr@{}}
    \toprule
    Mode                  & Encoder         & Predictor          & Head                 & Params tuned             \\
    \midrule
    Frozen probe          & frozen          & frozen             & LN + linear          & 513                      \\
    \textbf{Predictor-FT} & \textbf{frozen} & \textbf{finetuned} & \textbf{LN + linear} & \textbf{$\approx$\,198K} \\
    E2E                   & finetuned       & finetuned          & LN + linear          & $\approx$\,2.16M         \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predictor is run at sorted horizons $\mathcal{H} = \{{\Delta t}_1 < \cdots < {\Delta t}_K\}$. A shared linear head maps each predicted representation to a per-interval \emph{conditional hazard}:
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_k(t) \;=\; \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \hat{\h}_{(t, t+\Delta t_k]} + b\bigr) \;\in\; (0, 1),
  \label{eq:hazard}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_k(t) = P(\text{event in } (\Delta t_{k-1}, \Delta t_k] \mid T^* > \Delta t_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_{\leq t})$ and $T^*$ is the time-to-next-event. No distributional assumptions are required: each $\lambda_k$ is a free function of $\h_t$ via the predictor network. The event probability surface is the discrete-time survival CDF~\citep{lee2018deephit,ren2019drsa}:
\begin{equation}
  p(t, \Delta t_k) \;=\; 1 - \prod_{j=1}^{k} (1 - \lambda_j(t)).
  \label{eq:event_prob}
\end{equation}
The finetuning loss sums positive-weighted binary cross-entropy over horizons:
\begin{equation}
  \loss_\text{FT} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w^+ \cdot \text{BCE}\bigl(p(t, \Delta t_k),\; y(t, \Delta t_k)\bigr),
  \label{eq:ft_loss}
\end{equation}
where $y(t, \Delta t) = \mathbb{1}[\text{event in } (t, t{+}\Delta t]]$ and $w^+ = N_{\text{neg}} / N_{\text{pos}}$. Two structural properties follow from the parameterization, not from post-hoc enforcement:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity by construction.} Each factor $(1 - \lambda_j) \in (0, 1)$, so the survival product $\prod_{j \leq k}(1 - \lambda_j)$ is non-increasing in $k$, and $p(t, \Delta t_k) = 1 - \prod(\cdot)$ is non-decreasing. Zero violations by design.
  \item \textbf{Interval arithmetic.} $P(\text{event in } (t{+}a,\; t{+}b]) = p(t, b) - p(t, a)$ for $b > a$, since $p$ is a valid CDF.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:metrics}

The model outputs $p(t, \Delta t)$ (\cref{eq:event_prob}) for each observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$. This probability surface is the complete prediction; every metric we report - and every metric prior work reports - is a readout of it.

\subsection{Standard Metrics, Derived from the Surface}

We do not propose a new metric. Instead, we compute exactly the metrics each baseline uses - RMSE for C-MAPSS~\citep{fan2024star}, PA-F1 for anomaly benchmarks~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026}, AUROC for clinical datasets - \emph{from the same stored surface}. Every number in the ``Legacy (ours)'' column of \cref{tab:benchmark} is the standard metric for that domain, computed from our probability surface using the standard protocol, enabling direct head-to-head comparison.

The surface also supports a single cross-domain metric. Events are rare: turbine failure happens once per lifecycle; anomaly segments cover ${\sim}1{-}13\%$ of test data. We use \textbf{AUPRC} (area under the precision-recall curve), which is threshold-free and robust to class imbalance:
\begin{equation}
  \text{AUPRC} \;=\; \int_0^1 \text{Precision}(r) \, dr
  \label{eq:auprc}
\end{equation}
computed over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells pooled into a single ranked list. As a secondary metric, we report \textbf{AUROC} pooled over the same cells~\citep{davis2006auprc}. Crucially, AUPRC is not \emph{instead of} domain-specific metrics; it is \emph{in addition to} them.

\subsection{What We Report}

For each dataset we report: (1) the standard metric that prior work on that dataset uses (RMSE, PA-F1, AUROC), computed from our stored surface using the published protocol; (2) pooled AUPRC over all $(t, \Delta t)$ cells as a cross-domain summary; (3) AUROC as a secondary threshold-free metric. All results: mean $\pm$ std ($N$ seeds, 95\% CI). The probability surface is stored as \texttt{.npz} for every run, so any metric - including ones we did not anticipate - can be recomputed without re-running inference.

\subsection{Label Efficiency}
\label{sec:label_efficiency}

\textbf{Two regimes by dataset type.} The 10\%-label results (\cref{tab:benchmark}) reveal a clean two-regime structure. Lifecycle datasets, where a single entity undergoes a slow, predictable degradation trajectory, retain $\geq 97\%$ of full-label h-AUROC at 10\% labels: FD001 $0.772\pm0.059$ ($98\%$), FD003 $0.830\pm0.018$ ($97\%$), SMAP $0.580\pm0.047$ ($97\%$). The frozen encoder already captures the degradation trajectory from self-supervised pretraining; the finetuning objective only needs to steer the predictor toward the specific failure mode, a task that requires few labels when the signal is strong. In contrast, streaming-anomaly datasets depend on entity coverage: SMD uses only 3 of 28 entities at 10\% labels, falling near chance; MBA ($74\%$) truncates to 83 training windows. GECCO is excluded from lf10 comparisons because 10\% temporal truncation precedes the first positive label by design.

\textbf{Sub-5\% label efficiency.} We extend the analysis to $\{5\%, 2\%, 1\%\}$ labels on both FD001 and FD003 (see \cref{tab:sub5pct}). FD001 retains $93\%$ of full-label performance with only 5\% of training engines (4 engines), and $\mathbf{92\%}$ with just $\mathbf{2}$ engines out of 85 at 2\% labels ($0.724\pm0.013$). FD003 is more sensitive to entity coverage - a consequence of its 4 distinct fault modes requiring multiple representative engines - retaining 83\% at 5\% and 74\% at 2\%; at 1\% (single engine), variance is too high to be conclusive ($0.513\pm0.220$). The FD001 robustness is predicted by \cref{prop:retention}: pretraining already establishes $I(H_t; E_{t+\Delta t})$ from unlabeled data; downstream learning only needs to solve the mapping from representations to event probabilities.

\textbf{Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at low labels (legacy backbone).} Under the canonical backbone (198K predictor, AUPRC metric), pred-FT $0.9250\pm0.0005$ vs.\ E2E $0.9262\pm0.0008$ at 10\% labels ($\Delta{=}-0.001$); the modes are within $0.003$ AUPRC everywhere. The label-efficiency gap that was visible under the legacy backbone/F1w metric (\cref{app:label_efficiency_full}) does not survive the canonical architecture: RevIN, cumulative targets, and a smaller predictor tighten cross-seed variance. What pred-FT still buys is an ${\sim}11{\times}$ reduction in trainable parameters at matched AUPRC.

\paragraph{Additional ablations.} Predictor-inference contribution, pretrained vs.\ random-init predictor, EMA vs.\ SIGReg pretraining, and architecture sensitivity are in \cref{app:additional_ablations}. Key findings: (i)~pretrained predictor weights carry at most $+0.003$ AUPRC over random-init ($p > 0.08$ on all datasets); (ii)~SIGReg-pred pretraining improves F1w by $+0.06$ and RMSE by $-3.2$ over EMA on FD001.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Per-dataset pretraining.} We pretrain a separate encoder per dataset. Cross-domain pretraining on a union of datasets is the natural next step.
  \item \textbf{FD003 regression.} AUPRC drops to $0.766$ under the canonical $P{=}16$ architecture; the multi-fault degradation mode may need finer temporal resolution or more predictor capacity.
  \item \textbf{Chronos-2 parity on well-covered domains.} On datasets whose distribution is close to the Chronos-2 pretraining corpus, a 120M frozen encoder matches FAM. FAM's advantage is on out-of-distribution domains.
  \item \textbf{Anomaly ``prediction'' vs detection.} On SMAP, the model detects ongoing anomaly segments rather than predicting onset with lead time. True early warning requires lead-time-aware evaluation.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive tokenization.} We fix $P{=}16$ following PatchTST. The optimal patch size depends on sampling frequency and signal characteristics; a principled alternative would fix the token count (e.g., ${\sim}32$ tokens) and adapt $P$ to the context length.
\end{itemize}

% ============================================================
% 8. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

Time series foundation models optimize for forecasting: predicting the next values of every channel. But practitioners rarely care about the full future trajectory - they care about \emph{events}: will this turbine fail? Is this patient deteriorating? FAM inverts the priority: the encoder learns dynamics via self-supervised JEPA pretraining, then the predictor is steered toward the specific event that matters, using as few labels as needed. The probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$, parameterized as a discrete-time survival CDF, unifies degradation, anomaly, and clinical prediction under one threshold-free metric. Across 11 datasets in 7 domains, a lean 2.16M-parameter architecture outperforms Chronos-2 (120M parameters) on 7 of 8 comparable datasets when both use identical 198K-param downstream heads. Domain-specific JEPA pretraining produces better event-prediction representations than large-scale value-forecasting pretraining. The encoder learns what changes; the predictor learns what matters.

\section{PA-F1 Inflation}
\label{app:pa_f1}

PA-F1 (Point-Adjusted F1) credits an entire anomaly segment as correctly predicted if any single timestep within it exceeds the threshold~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}. This protocol inflates reported F1 dramatically when anomaly segments are long and prevalence is non-trivial: the TSAD-Eval study~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval} shows that a random detector can exceed $F_1 = 0.9$ PA on some datasets. We concretely demonstrate this by contrasting PA-F1 and non-PA F1 for FAM+Mahalanobis on our benchmarks: SMAP $0.793$ PA vs.\ $0.038$ non-PA, SMD $0.252$ PA vs.\ $0.144$ non-PA, PSM $0.813$ PA vs.\ $0.085$ non-PA. The gap in PA-F1 between FAM and MTS-JEPA remains large even under a random-baseline sanity check (a random-init encoder + Mahalanobis baseline achieves $0.604 \pm 0.007$ PA-F1 on SMAP; FAM is $0.79$), so the PA-F1 margin is not purely inflation - but any single PA-F1 number should be read in conjunction with the corresponding non-PA number.
