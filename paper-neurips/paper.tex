% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Mechanical failures---bearing seizures, turbine blade cracks, pump seal ruptures---are \emph{grey swans}: rare enough that labeled failure data is scarce, yet physically grounded enough that their signatures hide in abundant unlabeled operational sensor streams.
We ask whether self-supervised learning can extract these degradation signatures without failure labels, producing representations that generalize across fault types, operating conditions, and label budgets.
We introduce Trajectory JEPA, a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture that learns by predicting future multivariate sensor trajectories in latent space.
Our central empirical finding is that self-supervised trajectory prediction provides a large, quantifiable contribution to downstream RUL prediction: a from-scratch ablation (same architecture, same fine-tuning protocol, only the initialization changes) shows pretraining contributes $+8.8$ RMSE at 100\% labels and $+15.6$ RMSE at 10\% labels on NASA C-MAPSS FD001.
This label-efficiency advantage produces a crossover against the supervised state of the art: at 5\% of training engines, our frozen encoder ($21.53 \pm 2.0$) outperforms STAR ($24.55$)---a supervised architecture that achieves $12.19$ RMSE with full labels.
At 100\% labels the same encoder achieves $\rmse = 14.23 \pm 0.39$ on the canonical FD001 benchmark (5 seeds), outperforming a matched 5-seed replication of the only prior SSL baseline (AE-LSTM, $16.07 \pm 2.80$) by $1.84$ RMSE with $7\times$ lower seed variance, and showing genuine within-engine tracking confirmed by Spearman $\rho = 0.830 \pm 0.023$ and a $+41.5$ RMSE penalty when context vectors are shuffled across engines.
As a representation diagnostic, a linear Ridge probe on the frozen encoder recovers the C-MAPSS health index with $R^2 = 0.926$; because H.I.\ is a deterministic piecewise-linear transform of capped RUL, this quantifies the same encoder capability as frozen RMSE under a different evaluation protocol, and serves as supporting evidence for representation quality rather than a separate result.
A principled rationale connects the objective to slow feature analysis and an information-theoretic view of trajectory prediction. Architectural ablations (full-sequence target, cross-sensor attention) close up to $37\%$ of the frozen-probe gap to supervised SOTA at 100\% labels on FD001, with the full-sequence variant generalizing to FD003 and FD004. A head-to-head 5-seed replication of AE-LSTM on our pipeline gives $16.07 \pm 2.80$, against which Trajectory JEPA E2E ($14.23 \pm 0.39$) wins by $1.84$ RMSE with $7\times$ lower seed variance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A turbine blade cracks after 12,000 flight hours. A bearing seizes in a wind turbine gearbox during peak generation. A pump seal fails in a chemical plant, triggering an emergency shutdown. These are \emph{grey swans}---events that are rare in operational data but follow known degradation physics and are, in principle, predictable~\citep{greyswan_supplychain2025}. Unlike black swans, which are fundamentally unforeseeable~\citep{taleb2007blackswan}, grey swans are physically plausible failures that sit in the long tail of operating experience. They present a data paradox for prognostics: \emph{the events most critical to predict are precisely the ones with the least training data}.

Deep learning approaches for remaining useful life (RUL) prediction have shown strong results on benchmark datasets~\citep{lei2018machinery, zhao2019deeplearningphm}, with architectures ranging from CNN-LSTMs~\citep{zheng2017lstm} to Transformer-based models~\citep{fan2024star, ttsnet2025} achieving impressive in-domain accuracy. However, these methods require extensive labeled failure trajectories---run-to-failure sequences where degradation is observed from inception to failure---which are expensive, dangerous, and scarce by definition. Time series foundation models~\citep{das2024timesfm, ansari2024chronos, goswami2024moment} address data scarcity through massive pretraining, but optimize for next-value forecasting---a fundamentally different objective from encoding degradation state. A perfect one-step-ahead predictor of raw sensor amplitude tells us nothing about remaining useful life.

We take a different approach: rather than forecasting individual sensor values, we train an encoder to predict \emph{future sensor trajectories in latent space}. The key insight is that degradation affects what a sensor trajectory will look like in the near future---bearing frequencies shift, turbofan efficiency declines, vibration patterns change. An encoder forced to predict these trajectories must internalize the state variables that govern degradation dynamics, even without any labels.

Concretely, we introduce Trajectory JEPA: a causal Transformer encoder that, given sensor observations $\x_{1:t}$, predicts the latent representation of future observations $\x_{t+1:t+k}$ produced by an exponential moving average (EMA) target encoder~\citep{grill2020byol, assran2023ijepa}. The prediction is in latent space, not sensor space, avoiding the reconstruction bias of masked autoencoders~\citep{he2022mae} that favors low-level statistics over slow-varying degradation structure.

\paragraph{Structural supervision honesty.} All C-MAPSS training engines are run-to-failure, so the temporal structure of the data implicitly reveals degradation progression even though no explicit RUL labels are used during pretraining. This is weaker than labeled RUL supervision but stronger than truly unlabeled data with censored trajectories. We flag this throughout and quantify what the encoder actually learns via length-vs-content ablations (\cref{sec:representation}) and a from-scratch comparison (\cref{sec:from_scratch}); see also \cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Quantified pretraining contribution (from-scratch ablation).} Holding the architecture and E2E fine-tuning protocol fixed, pretrained initialization outperforms random initialization by $+8.8$ RMSE at 100\% labels and $+15.6$ RMSE at 10\% labels on FD001. This isolates the contribution of trajectory-prediction pretraining from the contribution of the Transformer architecture itself, and is the strongest quantitative evidence in this paper that the SSL objective---not the inductive biases of the model---carries the signal (\cref{sec:label_efficiency,sec:from_scratch}).

  \item \textbf{Label-efficiency crossover against supervised SOTA.} At 5\% of training engines, our frozen encoder (RMSE $21.53 \pm 2.0$) outperforms a 5-seed replication of STAR (RMSE $24.55$), the supervised state of the art that achieves $12.19$ RMSE with full labels. At 20\% labels, JEPA frozen ($19.83$) roughly matches STAR ($19.21$). This crossover in the grey-swan regime is the practical consequence of label-efficient self-supervised pretraining (\cref{sec:label_efficiency}).

  \item \textbf{Verified degradation tracking.} We rigorously verify that the benchmark RMSE ($14.23 \pm 0.39$ on FD001, 5 seeds) reflects genuine within-engine tracking, not population-mean regression: median within-engine Spearman $\rho = 0.830 \pm 0.023$, a $+41.5$ RMSE penalty under representation shuffling, and a 5.4 RMSE margin over a tight 58-feature hand-engineered baseline (\cref{sec:verification}). This five-diagnostic tracking protocol is, to our knowledge, the first rigorous verification of within-engine signal on C-MAPSS.

  \item \textbf{Representation diagnostic via H.I.\ recovery.} A linear Ridge probe on frozen representations recovers the piecewise-linear health index with validation $R^2 = 0.926$. Because H.I.\ is a deterministic transform of capped RUL (\cref{eq:hi_rul_equivalence}), this measures the same frozen-encoder capability as the 17.81 RMSE frozen probe under a different evaluation protocol (engine-level cycle-by-cycle Ridge regression on in-distribution validation engines, rather than last-window prediction on the canonical test set). We present the $R^2$ as supporting evidence for representation quality, not as a separate headline claim (\cref{sec:hi_recovery}).

  \item \textbf{Multi-subset generalization.} The approach generalizes across C-MAPSS subsets: FD003 (mixed fault modes, RMSE $15.37$), FD004 (6 operating conditions + 2 fault modes, RMSE $25.62$), and cross-fault transfer FD001$\to$FD003 at low labels outperforms a supervised LSTM (\cref{sec:multisubset}).

  \item \textbf{Theoretical rationale.} A principled sketch connecting trajectory prediction to Slow Feature Analysis~\citep{wiskott2002sfa}, an information-theoretic argument for why the encoder concentrates representational capacity on the slow (degradation) component, and an explanation for the frozen-vs-E2E tracking asymmetry (\cref{sec:theory_frozen_e2e}).

  \item \textbf{Architectural ablations.} Two architectural variants - a full-sequence target encoder (\cref{sec:full_sequence}) and a sensor-as-token cross-sensor attention encoder (\cref{sec:cross_sensor}) - are studied under the trajectory-prediction objective. The full-sequence target uniformly improves the frozen probe across FD001, FD003, and FD004 ($-2.1$, $-0.9$, $-1.3$ RMSE), and produces the best JEPA E2E on FD003 ($13.67 \pm 0.28$, down from $15.37$). Cross-sensor attention gives the best FD001 frozen result ($14.98 \pm 0.22$) but does not generalize to FD003 and is brittle at low labels; we report it as an FD001 finding. Both wins are SSL-contingent: from-scratch ablation on cross-sensor shows pretraining contributes $+21.5$ RMSE at 10\% labels.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Self-Supervised Learning for Time Series}
\label{sec:related_ssl}

Self-supervised pretraining for time series has followed two paradigms: contrastive and generative.

\paragraph{Contrastive methods.} \citet{chen2020simclr} established the contrastive learning paradigm with SimCLR; subsequent work adapted it to time series. \citet{yue2022ts2vec} learn representations via hierarchical contrastive learning across temporal and instance dimensions. \citet{tonekaboni2021tnc} exploit the stationarity of temporal neighborhoods, defining positive pairs within stationary windows---an assumption violated by degradation data, which is fundamentally non-stationary. \citet{eldele2021tstcc} combine temporal and contextual contrasting, while \citet{zhang2022tfc} align time-domain and frequency-domain representations, demonstrating cross-domain transfer on medical and mechanical datasets. Of these, TF-C is closest to our setting, but evaluates on fault \emph{classification}, not RUL regression.

\paragraph{Masked reconstruction.} Masked autoencoders~\citep{he2022mae} established that high masking ratios (75\%) force encoders to learn semantic structure rather than local interpolation. \citet{nie2023patchtst} adapt the Vision Transformer to time series via channel-independent patching and masked pretraining. \citet{dong2023simmtm} reconstruct masked time points from multiple masked versions, and \citet{cheng2023timemae} combine masked autoencoders with decoupled representation learning. These methods optimize for reconstruction fidelity, which biases representations toward low-level signal statistics rather than degradation-relevant features. Our JEPA objective avoids this bias by predicting in latent space rather than signal space~\citep{assran2023ijepa}.

\paragraph{EMA-based methods.} Our architecture uses an EMA target encoder to prevent representation collapse. This mechanism originates in MoCo~\citep{he2020moco} and DINO~\citep{caron2021dino}, and was shown by \citet{grill2020byol} (BYOL) to remove the need for negative pairs. \citet{bardes2022vicreg} formalize this through variance-invariance-covariance regularization. \citet{balestriero2025lejepa} further ground JEPA training theoretically with the provably optimal SIGReg regularizer.

\paragraph{Predictive coding.} Our trajectory prediction objective---predicting future latent representations from past context---is conceptually related to Contrastive Predictive Coding~\citep{oord2018cpc}. The key distinction is architectural: CPC uses a contrastive InfoNCE loss requiring negative samples, while JEPA uses direct regression in the target encoder's space with EMA stabilization. Both share the insight that predicting the future in latent space forces the encoder to capture slow-varying structure---in our case, degradation dynamics.

\subsection{Remaining Useful Life Prediction}
\label{sec:related_rul}

RUL prediction has progressed from physics-based models to deep learning. Early work applied CNNs~\citep{li2018dcnn} and LSTMs~\citep{zheng2017lstm} to sensor data; modern architectures combine these with attention. On C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss}, the current supervised state of the art is STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (FD001 RMSE: 10.61). TTS-Net~\citep{ttsnet2025} fuses Transformer, TCN, and self-attention branches (FD001 RMSE: 11.02). Nearly all methods are fully supervised.

\paragraph{Self-supervised pretraining for RUL.} \citet{lecam2025aelstm} train an autoencoder LSTM to reconstruct sensor sequences on C-MAPSS, achieving FD001 RMSE 13.99---the only published SSL result prior to our work. \citet{ding2022ssl_rul} propose multi-modal augmentation-based pretraining for rotating machinery, while \citet{wang2024masked_rul} apply masked autoencoders to turbofan sensors. None evaluate representation quality independently of the downstream metric (e.g., via health index recovery or within-engine tracking correlation); our work provides this analysis.

\paragraph{Health index construction.} Learning a health indicator from sensor data is related to our health index recovery result. \citet{guo2017healthindex} construct bearing health indicators via RNNs. \citet{wang2022healthindex} propose unsupervised multi-criteria methods. \citet{li2024hi_ssl} apply contrastive learning for health index construction. These methods require degradation labels or expert thresholds. Our result is distinct: the health index emerges as a linear readout of frozen SSL representations ($R^2 = 0.926$) without any health labels---the trajectory prediction task alone is sufficient.

\subsection{Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures}
\label{sec:related_jepa}

JEPA learns by predicting representations of masked inputs from visible context in a shared latent space~\citep{lecun2022path}. \citet{assran2023ijepa} demonstrate that predicting in representation space avoids the reconstruction bias of MAEs. \citet{bardes2024vjepa} extend to video with 90\% spatiotemporal masking. \citet{wang2024brainjepa} apply JEPA to fMRI brain dynamics with domain-specific positioning (NeurIPS 2024 Spotlight)---the closest analogue to our work in adapting JEPA to a non-vision domain. For time series, \citet{tsjepa2024} adapt JEPA with temporal masking for classification, while \citet{mtsjepa2026} introduce codebook regularization for multivariate series. \emph{None of these evaluate on prognostics or RUL tasks.} Our work is the first to apply JEPA to RUL prediction and to show that the learned representations linearly decode a latent health variable.

\paragraph{Linear probing as evaluation.} We evaluate representation quality using linear probes~\citep{alain2017probes}, the standard diagnostic in SSL~\citep{chen2020simclr, grill2020byol}. A frozen encoder whose representations support strong linear readout has genuinely internalized task-relevant structure during pretraining, rather than relying on nonlinear adaptation during fine-tuning.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

Consider a set of run-to-failure episodes $\{(\x_i^{(1)}, \ldots, \x_i^{(T_i)})\}_{i=1}^N$, where $\x_i^{(t)} \in \R^{S}$ is a vector of $S$ sensor readings at cycle $t$ (e.g., $S = 14$ selected sensors for C-MAPSS), and $T_i$ is the total lifetime of engine $i$. We define the piecewise-linear capped remaining useful life:
\begin{equation}
  \rul(t) = \min(T_i - t, \; R_{\max}),
  \label{eq:rul}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\max} = 125$ cycles reflects the ``healthy plateau'' convention: early-life cycles where the engine is far from failure are assigned a constant RUL, preventing the model from wasting capacity on a regime where degradation has not yet begun~\citep{zheng2017lstm}.


\subsection{Trajectory JEPA Pretraining}
\label{sec:jepa}

Trajectory JEPA learns by predicting future sensor trajectories in latent space, without failure labels. Given the past sensor observations $\x_{1:t}$, the model predicts the latent representation of future observations $\x_{t+1:t+k}$.

\paragraph{Context encoder.} The context encoder $\encoder$ is a causal Transformer ($d_\text{model} = 256$, 2 layers, 4 heads, 1.26M parameters). Each sensor vector $\x^{(t)} \in \R^S$ is projected to a $d$-dimensional token via a linear layer, with sinusoidal positional encoding indexed by cycle number. The encoder uses a causal attention mask, processing the sequence autoregressively:
\begin{equation}
  \h_\text{past} = \encoder(\x_{1:t}) \in \R^d,
  \label{eq:context}
\end{equation}
where $\h_\text{past}$ is the hidden state at the last observed cycle---a $d$-dimensional summary of the engine's history up to time $t$.

\paragraph{Target encoder.} The target encoder $\target$ is an EMA copy of $\encoder$ with momentum $\tau = 0.99$:
\begin{equation}
  \xi \leftarrow \tau \xi + (1 - \tau) \theta.
  \label{eq:ema}
\end{equation}
It encodes the future sensor window $\x_{t+1:t+k}$ bidirectionally (no causal mask) with attention pooling to produce $\h_\text{future} \in \R^d$.

\paragraph{Horizon-aware predictor.} The predictor $\predictor$ takes $\h_\text{past}$ and the prediction horizon $k$ to produce $\hat{\h}_\text{future}$. The horizon $k$ is sampled uniformly from $[5, 30]$ during training, so the predictor must learn how degradation evolves across multiple timescales.

\paragraph{Training objective.} We minimize the L1 distance between predicted and target future representations:
\begin{equation}
  \loss_{\text{JEPA}} = \| \predictor(\h_\text{past}, k) - \text{sg}[\target(\x_{t+1:t+k})] \|_1,
  \label{eq:jepa_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\text{sg}[\cdot]$ is stop-gradient. The EMA target encoder and the L1 loss together prevent representation collapse without requiring an explicit regularization term~\citep{grill2020byol, bardes2022vicreg}. Pretraining runs for 200 epochs with probe-based early stopping (best probe RMSE at epoch 50).

\paragraph{Pretraining data.} We pretrain on the C-MAPSS training engines (85 engines after an 85\%/15\% train/val split). For each engine, we sample 20 random cut points, yielding $\sim$1,700 (past, future) pairs per epoch. \emph{No RUL labels or failure indicators are used during pretraining.}

\subsection{Downstream Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:finetune}

After pretraining, a linear RUL probe maps $\h_\text{past} \to \hat{\rul}$. We evaluate two modes:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Frozen:} encoder weights are locked; only the linear probe trains. This measures representation quality directly---the probe is deliberately simple~\citep{alain2017probes}.
  \item \textbf{E2E (end-to-end):} encoder and probe train jointly with a lower encoder learning rate ($10^{-4}$ vs.\ $10^{-3}$ for the probe) to preserve pretrained structure while allowing task-specific adaptation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Health Index Probing}
\label{sec:hi_method}

To evaluate whether the encoder has learned degradation structure \emph{independent of any labeled objective}, we define an approximate health index (H.I.) for each training engine:
\begin{equation}
  \text{H.I.}(t) = \begin{cases} 1.0 & \text{if } t \leq T_i - R_{\max} \\ 1 - \frac{t - (T_i - R_{\max})}{R_{\max}} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
  \label{eq:hi}
\end{equation}
This piecewise-linear function is 1.0 during the healthy phase and declines linearly to 0 at failure, matching the RUL-cap convention. Note that H.I.\ is a deterministic affine transform of capped RUL:
\begin{equation}
  \text{H.I.}(t) = \frac{\min(T_i - t, R_{\max})}{R_{\max}} = \frac{\rul(t)}{R_{\max}},
  \label{eq:hi_rul_equivalence}
\end{equation}
so a probe that predicts H.I.\ is predicting a rescaled RUL and measures the same underlying representation capability as the RUL probe.

We fit a Ridge regression ($\alpha = 1.0$) from frozen $\h_\text{past}$ embeddings to H.I.\ targets on training engines, and evaluate $R^2$ on held-out validation engines. A high $R^2$ means the encoder's frozen representations contain degradation state in a linearly accessible form. We use $R^2$ as a diagnostic of representation quality, reported alongside the frozen-probe RMSE rather than as a separate claim.

At $100\%$ labels STAR wins by $2.0$ RMSE; at $20\%$ the gap to JEPA E2E is only $0.3$ RMSE; at $10\%$ it is $1.3$ RMSE; at $5\%$ JEPA frozen ($21.53 \pm 2.0$) outperforms STAR ($24.55 \pm 6.5$) by $3.0$ RMSE and has $3\times$ lower seed variance. The crossover is the practical consequence of label-efficient self-supervised pretraining: STAR's advantage is consumed by seed variance as labels shrink, while the frozen encoder is stable.

\paragraph{Beating the hand-engineered baseline.} The 58-feature Ridge regressor (RMSE $19.21$) is the tightest ``non-learning'' lower bound: it uses per-engine summary statistics (sensor means, slopes, length) but is flat within each engine. JEPA E2E outperforms it by $5.0$ RMSE, confirming the learned representation captures information beyond cross-engine summary statistics.

The $R^2$ value is numerically higher for two reasons beyond representation quality: (i) H.I.\ is evaluated cycle-by-cycle across all training and validation cycles, so the $\sim$40\% of cycles that lie on the healthy plateau ($\text{H.I.}=1$) are trivially predictable by a constant bias and inflate $R^2$; (ii) evaluation is on in-distribution validation engines (15\% holdout from the training set) rather than on the harder canonical test set used for RMSE. We therefore treat the high $R^2$ as confirmation that the frozen representation is linearly degradation-aligned, not as a separate claim that outperforms the RMSE result.

\subsection{Verification: Is the Tracking Genuine?}
\label{sec:verification}

A central concern with RUL benchmarks is that the canonical last-window protocol (one prediction per test engine) can be satisfied by cross-engine regression without within-engine tracking. We verify tracking via five independent diagnostics (\cref{fig:cmapss_tracking}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/v12_cmapss_tracking.pdf}
\caption{Within-engine tracking evidence (5 seeds). \textbf{(a)} Per-engine prediction std distribution; median $= 12.1 \pm 0.7$ (far above constant-predictor threshold of 3). \textbf{(b)} Within-engine Spearman $\rho$ distribution; median $= 0.830 \pm 0.023$, with 63/100 engines achieving $\rho > 0.7$. \textbf{(c)} Shuffle test: swapping $\h_\text{past}$ across engines degrades RMSE from 14.0 to 55.4 ($+41$ cycles).}
\label{fig:cmapss_tracking}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Prediction variance.} Per-engine prediction standard deviation, median $= 12.1 \pm 0.7$ cycles (5 seeds). A constant predictor has std $\approx 0$; a tracker should have std $10$--$30$. \textbf{PASS.}
  \item \textbf{Within-engine Spearman $\rho$.} Median $\rho = 0.830 \pm 0.023$ between predicted and true RUL across all cycles of each test engine. 63/100 engines have $\rho > 0.7$ on average. \textbf{PASS.}
  \item \textbf{Shuffle test.} Randomly permuting $\h_\text{past}$ across engines at inference time degrades RMSE from 13.98 to $55.45 \pm 1.62$. The $+41.5$ penalty proves $\h_\text{past}$ carries engine-specific degradation information. \textbf{PASS.}
  \item \textbf{Hand-engineered baseline margin.} JEPA E2E outperforms the 58-feature Ridge regressor (which is flat within each engine) by 5.0 RMSE. The benchmark requires genuine within-engine signal. \textbf{PASS.}
  \item \textbf{Sliding-cut evaluation.} Evaluating across all cut points (not just the last window) gives RMSE $11.77$---15\% better than last-window ($13.98$), as expected from a genuine tracker that benefits from having more history at earlier cut points (\cref{fig:cmapss_main}c). \textbf{PASS.}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Label Efficiency and Comparability of Baselines}
\label{sec:label_efficiency}

The grey swan motivation is that failure data is scarce. \Cref{tab:main_results,fig:v14_label_fromscratch}a show the full label-efficiency sweep. At 5\% labels (4 training engines), JEPA frozen achieves RMSE $21.53 \pm 2.0$ while the supervised LSTM collapses to $33.08 \pm 9.6$ and STAR to $24.55 \pm 6.5$. The frozen encoder's variance is $5$--$30\times$ lower than the LSTM and $3\times$ lower than STAR at the smallest budgets.

To resolve this, we replicated AE-LSTM in our pipeline (reconstruction autoencoder FC $14 \to 64 \to 32 \to 32 \to 64 \to 14$ followed by an LSTM regressor with $\text{hidden}=64$, joint fine-tuning, our splits and RUL cap). 5 seeds, fixed hyperparameters:

On FD003 the biggest healthy-to-degradation attention shifts point at $s_8$ (physical fan speed $N_f$) and $s_{13}$ (corrected fan speed $N_{Rf}$) rather than $s_{14}$, and are \emph{negative} in sign (attention \emph{defocuses} away from fan-speed sensors during degradation). In both subsets the focal sensors are rotor-speed related - $s_8$, $s_{13}$, or $s_{14}$ - but the specific sensor and direction depend on fault mode. The FD003 frozen RMSE under the cross-sensor architecture is $24.49 \pm 0.12$ vs.\ V2 frozen $19.25$, so the architectural improvement is also FD001-specific. We therefore report cross-sensor as an FD001 finding rather than a universal improvement; attention-map figures (both subsets) are in the supplementary material.

\subsection{From-Scratch Ablation}
\label{sec:from_scratch}

The from-scratch row in \cref{tab:main_results} and the right panel of \cref{fig:v14_label_fromscratch} isolate the pretraining contribution by holding the architecture and fine-tuning protocol fixed. Delta (scratch $-$ pretrained) is $+8.8$ at 100\%, $+14.5$ at 20\%, $+15.6$ at 10\%, and $+8.0$ at 5\% (the 5\% delta shrinks because both pretrained E2E and scratch E2E degrade; the frozen probe remains the strongest option at that budget, at $24.47$). This is the strongest quantitative evidence in the paper that the SSL objective, rather than the Transformer architecture alone, carries the signal.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol Transparency}
\label{sec:eval_protocol}

The standard C-MAPSS evaluation tests each engine at a single cut point (the last observed cycle). We additionally report sliding-cut RMSE (all cut points from cycle 30 to end), which more honestly measures how the model performs across the full operational range. The sliding-cut RMSE ($11.77$) is better than last-window ($13.98$), as expected: at earlier cut points, the encoder has more history and tracks degradation more precisely. We advocate publishing both metrics.

\subsection{What Does the Encoder Represent?}
\label{sec:representation}

\paragraph{PCA analysis.} PC1 of the frozen encoder embeddings explains 47.6\% of variance with $|\rho| = 0.797$ against the health index. The representation is low-rank and degradation-aligned.

\paragraph{Frozen vs.\ E2E.} The frozen encoder has \emph{higher} tracking $\rho$ (0.856) than E2E (0.804). This is surprising: E2E, which sees RUL labels, tracks degradation \emph{less} faithfully. The explanation is that E2E optimizes for RMSE calibration (mapping to the correct scale) at the expense of rank preservation. The degradation signal originates entirely from pretraining.

\subsection{Theoretical Rationale}
\label{sec:theory_frozen_e2e}

We offer a principled sketch (not a formal theorem) of why trajectory-prediction pretraining produces degradation-aligned representations. A complete derivation is in the supplementary material.

\paragraph{Slow feature bias.} The predictor $\predictor(\h_\text{past}, k)$ cannot reduce the conditional innovation of a feature: the component of $\h_\text{future}$ that is unpredictable from $\h_\text{past}$. Features with low temporal innovation (slow-varying) are therefore rewarded by the L1 prediction loss, while features with high innovation (fast-varying noise, operating-condition oscillations) are penalized. Mechanical degradation is the slowly-varying latent variable; the L1 objective thus biases the encoder toward representing it. This is the same inductive bias that Slow Feature Analysis~\citep{wiskott2002sfa} formalizes in the covariance domain.

\paragraph{Information-theoretic view.} Under the assumptions (A1) $\x^{(t)} = f(\text{H.I.}(t), \varepsilon_t)$ with H.I.\ slow and $\varepsilon$ fast i.i.d.\ noise, (A2) a smooth H.I.\ dynamics (so $\text{H.I.}(t+k) \mid \text{H.I.}(t)$ is concentrated), the mutual information $I(\x_{1:t}; \x_{t+1:t+k})$ is dominated by the H.I.\ component. Any predictor maximizing $I(\h_\text{past}; \h_\text{future})$ must concentrate on the H.I.\ channel. JEPA's L1 loss is not literally an MI bound but shares the same incentive structure as CPC~\citep{oord2018cpc}, which is.

\paragraph{Why frozen tracks better than E2E.} About $40\%$ of training labels sit at the capped RUL plateau. The MSE gradient under this label distribution has a large ``predict the plateau'' component, which biases E2E fine-tuning toward calibrating the healthy regime at the expense of rank preservation during degradation. The frozen encoder is uncontaminated by this label-induced gradient bias, which explains $\rho_\text{frozen} = 0.856 > \rho_\text{E2E} = 0.804$.

\paragraph{Length vs.\ content.} The encoder receives sensor values and sinusoidal positional encodings indexed by cycle number. Since all training engines are run-to-failure, sequence length correlates with elapsed life, so in principle the encoder could exploit length as a shortcut. Three inference-only tests on the frozen V2 encoder rule this out: (1)~a constant-input test (repeat one cycle's sensors $t$ times for $t \in [30, 200]$) produces a prediction range of only $2.3$ cycles, showing that the PE does not dominate the output; (2)~a length-matched cross-engine swap (same $t$, different sensor content) gives mean cosine similarity $0.647$ between representations, well below 1; (3)~a temporal shuffle (permuting cycle order within each engine) collapses RMSE from $16.1$ to $36.8$ and within-engine $\rho$ from $0.896$ to $0.771$. The encoder reads temporal sensor content, not sequence length (see \cref{sec:from_scratch} for the from-scratch ablation, which reaches the same conclusion from a different angle).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Run-to-failure assumption.} All C-MAPSS training engines run to failure. The temporal structure of the pretraining data implicitly reveals degradation progression: the encoder sees trajectories that always end at failure, and sequence length correlates with degradation state. This is weaker than labeled RUL supervision, but stronger than truly unlabeled data (e.g., censored industrial data where engines are replaced before failure). The length-vs-content ablation (\cref{sec:representation}) and the from-scratch comparison (\cref{sec:from_scratch}) together confirm that the encoder reads sensor content, not merely sequence length; but we note the structural supervision as a genuine qualifier on the "no labels" claim.

  \item \textbf{Single benchmark.} Results are on C-MAPSS, a simulation with known physics. Generalization to real industrial sensors with noise, missing data, and sensor drift is untested.

  \item \textbf{Gap to supervised SOTA.} JEPA E2E achieves 14.23 vs.\ STAR 12.19 ($\sim$2 RMSE gap). Closing this gap likely requires architectural scaling or improved fine-tuning schedules.

  \item \textbf{FD002/FD004 multi-condition challenge.} The per-condition normalization fails under distribution shift. Condition-aware encoder designs (condition tokens, conditioned normalization) are needed.

  \item \textbf{Bearing vibration data.} We also evaluated JEPA on single-channel bearing vibration from FEMTO~\citep{nectoux2012pronostia} and XJTU-SY~\citep{wang2020xjtusybearing} (23 run-to-failure episodes, 8 pretraining sources, 33{,}939 windows). JEPA alone achieved weak correlation with the dominant degradation indicator (spectral centroid PC1 $\rho = 0.071$) and required fusion with handcrafted spectral features to be competitive (Appendix~\ref{app:bearings}). This highlights a data-scale limitation: 23 bearing episodes may be insufficient for self-supervised pretraining to discover degradation structure that domain experts encode via spectral centroid, kurtosis, and envelope analysis. The C-MAPSS results (100--260 engines with 14 sensors) suggest the framework becomes effective at larger data scales with richer multivariate structure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced Trajectory JEPA, a self-supervised framework for mechanical prognostics in the grey swan regime. The central empirical finding is that trajectory-prediction pretraining contributes a large and quantifiable fraction of downstream RUL performance: in a matched-architecture ablation, pretraining improves RMSE by $+8.8$ at 100\% labels and $+15.6$ at 10\% labels on FD001. The practical consequence is a label-efficiency crossover: at 5\% of training engines, the frozen self-supervised encoder outperforms a 5-seed replication of the supervised state of the art. Tracking is verified by five independent diagnostics including a $+41.5$ RMSE shuffle penalty and Spearman $\rho = 0.830$; as a representation diagnostic, a linear probe on the frozen encoder recovers the simulator's health index at $R^2 = 0.926$.

The frozen encoder exhibits \emph{higher} tracking fidelity than the E2E fine-tuned version, suggesting that the pretraining objective itself is the source of the degradation signal; fine-tuning refines calibration rather than detection. At low label budgets, this translates to $5$--$30\times$ lower variance than supervised approaches---critical for industrial deployment where consistency matters.

The length-vs-content ablation (constant-input, length-matched swap, and temporal-shuffle tests) confirms the encoder reads sensor content rather than primarily encoding sequence length. All training data is nonetheless run-to-failure, which provides implicit structural supervision at the pretraining stage; we flag this in \cref{sec:limitations}. The C-MAPSS community has not previously published within-engine tracking diagnostics, sliding-cut evaluation, or tight feature-regressor baselines alongside last-window RMSE, and we hope the honest reporting of both strong results and open loopholes is itself a contribution. \plannedc{Open directions include architectural study (cross-sensor attention, multi-scale prediction targets) and a theoretical analysis linking trajectory prediction to slow feature analysis.}

\section{C-MAPSS Dataset Details}
\label{app:cmapss}

C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} simulates turbofan engine degradation under controlled conditions. Each engine starts with slightly different initial wear and operates until failure. The simulation produces 21 sensor channels per cycle; we select 14 informative sensors (dropping 7 near-constant channels: s1, s5, s6, s10, s16, s18, s19).

For FD001/FD003 (single operating condition): global min-max normalization per sensor on training data only. For FD002/FD004 (6 operating conditions): per-condition normalization via KMeans clustering ($k=6$) on operating settings, with min-max applied per cluster. Test data is normalized using training statistics only.

Cross-fault transfer incurs 7--9 RMSE penalty, but the cross-fault probe at 10\% labels (28.79) still outperforms a supervised LSTM at the same budget (31.22), demonstrating that SSL representations provide useful structure even across mismatched fault modes.

Cross-subset pretraining (FD002$\to$FD001) matches or slightly outperforms FD001 in-domain frozen at high labels (100\%: 17.50 vs 17.81) but underperforms at low labels (10\%: 24.45 vs 19.93), indicating that more data from a different distribution does not substitute for matched pretraining data at small scale.

\section{Bearing Vibration Experiments}
\label{app:bearings}

To assess whether the framework generalizes to single-channel vibration data at smaller scale, we also evaluated on bearing run-to-failure datasets.

\paragraph{Setup.} JEPA pretraining on 33,939 vibration windows from 8 bearing sources (CWRU, MFPT, IMS, FEMTO, XJTU-SY, Paderborn, Ottawa, MAFAULDA), all resampled to 12,800\,Hz. The encoder is a 4-layer ViT ($d = 256$, 3.2M parameters) operating on 16 patches of 64 samples each, with 62.5\% masking. Evaluation on FEMTO~\citep{nectoux2012pronostia} and XJTU-SY~\citep{wang2020xjtusybearing} (23 episodes, 75\%/25\% split).

\paragraph{Results.} JEPA alone (RMSE 0.189) matches handcrafted features (HC+LSTM: 0.177, $p = 0.40$) but has very low correlation with the dominant degradation indicator (spectral centroid PC1 $\rho = 0.071$). A hybrid architecture fusing JEPA embeddings with 18 handcrafted spectral features achieves RMSE $0.055 \pm 0.004$ ($+75.5\%$ vs.\ time-only baseline), confirming that JEPA captures complementary information. For cross-dataset transfer (FEMTO$\to$XJTU-SY), temporal contrastive pretraining outperforms JEPA by $+18.8\%$ ($p < 0.001$).

\paragraph{Lesson.} The bearing results highlight a data-scale limitation. With 23 run-to-failure episodes and single-channel vibration, JEPA pretraining does not discover spectral centroid shift---the dominant degradation indicator---that domain experts encode directly. The framework requires either (a)~larger datasets with richer structure (as in C-MAPSS) or (b)~domain-informed masking strategies that direct the encoder toward degradation-relevant features.
