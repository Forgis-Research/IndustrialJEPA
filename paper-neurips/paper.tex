% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
  Forecasting events in multivariate time series -- a turbine blade cracking, a bearing degrading, a server spiking, a cardiac rhythm shifting -- demands methods that generalise across event types and domains, especially when labeled failures are scarce. Current methods couple the event definition to the model architecture: each event type requires its own supervised pipeline. We introduce FAM (Forecast-Anything Model), which separates \emph{learning dynamics} from \emph{learning events}. A causal encoder pretrained via JEPA learns to forecast future representations from unlabeled data alone; downstream, we freeze the encoder and finetune only a small horizon-conditioned predictor toward the event of interest, output as a discrete-time survival CDF. Because the encoder already captures precursor dynamics during pretraining, as few as 2\% of labels suffice to reach near-full-label performance -- and our information-theoretic analysis shows this holds whenever the future interval contains detectable precursors (\cref{sec:theory}). One architecture handles 11 datasets across 8 domains, and outperforms time-series foundation models on 6 of 8 comparable benchmarks at $56{\times}$ fewer parameters, when both use identical downstream heads. Code and stored probability surfaces at \url{https://github.com/Forgis-Research/IndustrialJEPA}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_hero.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{One model, any event, scarce labels.} (a)~FAM at 10\% labels (open circles) matches or exceeds Chronos-2 at 100\% labels (diamonds) on most datasets. (b)~Predicted $p(t, \Delta t)$ surfaces on turbofan degradation (C-MAPSS) and cardiac arrhythmia (MBA).}
  \label{fig:hero}

A turbine blade cracks after 12{,}000 flight hours. A bearing degrades over weeks of vibration data. A satellite sensor drifts silently for 48 hours. These events are rare in operational data, yet they follow partially predictable precursor dynamics~\citep{taleb2007blackswan,scheffer2009earlywarning}. Current methods couple the event definition to the model architecture: RUL models~\citep{fan2024star} never see anomaly benchmarks; anomaly detectors~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026} never forecast time-to-failure.

Yet every event forecasting task has the same structure: given observations up to time $t$, estimate $P(\text{event within } \Delta t)$ for each horizon $\Delta t$. The \emph{encoder} that learns temporal dynamics does not need to know which event will matter downstream; it learns general structure from unlabeled data. The \emph{predictor}, finetuned with a small number of event labels, specialises the learned dynamics to whichever event matters.

\paragraph{FAM.} The key design choice is \emph{what} to forecast. Value-forecasting models, including foundation models like Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos} and TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, optimise for reconstructing future observations, so their representations are shaped by all variation in the signal -- including noise and dynamics irrelevant to the downstream event. JEPA~\citep{assran2023ijepa} forecasts future \emph{representations} with no reconstruction obligation: the encoder is free to learn a latent space that retains what is predictable about the future and discards what is not. Predictable precursor dynamics are preserved when the pretraining loss is sufficiently minimised; unpredictable variation is compressed away (\cref{sec:theory}). We apply this to time series: a causal Transformer encodes observations up to $t$ into $\h_t$; a horizon-conditioned predictor $\predictor$ maps $(\h_t, \Delta t)$ to a forecast future representation, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales. Pretraining uses standard components (patches~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, instance normalisation~\citep{kim2022revin}, causal attention, EMA target) and is self-supervised.

\paragraph{Core idea: predictor finetuning.} After pretraining, the standard JEPA recipe discards the predictor and trains a linear probe on the frozen encoder. We retain the predictor: freeze the encoder but finetune the predictor alongside a lightweight event head. The predictor is a small horizon-conditioned MLP (${\sim}9\%$ of parameters) that is more expressive than a linear probe but far cheaper than end-to-end finetuning. The output is structured as a discrete-time survival CDF~\citep{lee2018deephit,gensheimer2019nnetsurvival}, guaranteeing that event probabilities are monotonically non-decreasing in the prediction horizon by construction.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{One architecture, any event, any domain.} A single 2.16M-param architecture across 11 datasets in 8 domains, evaluated through a unified probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$, output as a discrete-time survival CDF that guarantees monotonic event probabilities by construction. With identical downstream heads, FAM wins 6 of 8 head-to-head comparisons against foundation models at $43$--$158{\times}$ fewer parameters (\cref{sec:metrics,sec:experiments}).

  \item \textbf{Predictor finetuning as the downstream recipe.} Freeze the encoder, finetune only the predictor + event head (198K params, ${\sim}11{\times}$ fewer than E2E). This simple recipe enables extreme label efficiency: 92\% of full-label performance with 2\% of labels, because the frozen encoder already carries the event-relevant dynamics from pretraining (\cref{sec:finetune_ablation,sec:label_efficiency}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Self-supervised learning for time series.}
Contrastive methods (TS2Vec~\citep{yue2022ts2vec}, TNC~\citep{tonekaboni2021tnc}, TimesURL~\citep{liu2024timesurl}) learn by contrasting pairs. Masked reconstruction (PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, SimMTM~\citep{dong2023simmtm}) reconstructs masked patches. JEPA predicts future \emph{representations}, avoiding reconstruction bias~\citep{assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}. For time series, TS-JEPA~\citep{tsjepa2024} applies temporal masking for classification; MTS-JEPA~\citep{mtsjepa2026} adds codebook regularisation for anomaly detection. All of these discard their pretraining head at inference and probe only the encoder. We keep the predictor and show it carries event-relevant signal.

\paragraph{Foundation models for time series.}
Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, and Moirai~\citep{woo2024unified} pretrain on large-scale corpora for generic value forecasting. Generative pretraining (Timer~\citep{liu2024timer}) and LLM repurposing (GPT4TS~\citep{zhou2023gpt4ts}) offer alternative transfer strategies. FAM targets a different output: event probabilities rather than future channel values. The encoder is mid-scale ($\approx$\,2.16M) and pretrained per-dataset rather than on a massive corpus; what transfers across domains is the \emph{recipe} (architecture + predictor finetuning), not the weights. We benchmark against all four foundation models above (\cref{sec:experiments,app:chronos_full,app:extra_baselines}).

\paragraph{Prognostics and anomaly prediction.}
C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} is the standard RUL benchmark; supervised SOTA: STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (RMSE 10.61). Anomaly prediction methods (Anomaly Transformer~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}, DCdetector~\citep{yang2023dcdetector}, TranAD~\citep{tuli2022tranad}) report point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which inflates scores by crediting entire segments from a single detection~\citep{kim2022rigorous,schmidl2022tsadeval}. We replace these incomparable metrics with AUPRC pooled over the probability surface (\cref{sec:metrics}).

\paragraph{Channel strategies.}
PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} favours channel-independence; iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} favours variate-as-token. We default to channel-fusion and ablate sensor-as-token (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{FAM architecture.} \emph{Top:} pretraining. $\encoder$ (causal) $\to$ $\h_t$; $\predictor(\h_t, \Delta t)$ $\to$ $\hat{\h}$; EMA target $\target$ provides L1 target $\h^*$ from $\x_{(t, t+\Delta t]}$. \emph{Bottom:} finetuning. Encoder frozen; predictor runs at $K$ horizons; hazard logits $\to$ $\lambda_k$ $\to$ cumulative product $\to$ survival CDF $p(t, \Delta t_k)$, monotonically non-decreasing by construction.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Context encoder} $\encoder$: a causal Transformer ($d{=}256$, 2 layers, 4 heads). Given observations $\x_{\leq t}$, it produces a summary embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \h_t = \encoder(\x_{\leq t}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Predictor} $\predictor$: a 2-layer MLP. Given $\h_t$ and a prediction horizon $\Delta t$, it produces a predicted embedding of the future interval $(t, t{+}\Delta t]$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} = \predictor(\h_t, \Delta t).
  \label{eq:predictor}
\end{equation}
During pretraining, $\Delta t \sim \text{LogU}[1, {\Delta t}_\text{max}]$ is sampled randomly, forcing the encoder to internalise dynamics at multiple timescales.

\textbf{Target encoder} $\target$: an EMA copy of $\encoder$ ($\tau{=}0.99$) that encodes $\x_{(t, t+\Delta t]}$ bidirectionally with attention pooling $\to \h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]} \in \R^d$. The target interval starts at $t$ with no gap; the same interval is used for event prediction downstream (\cref{sec:downstream}). We also ablate a SIGReg variant~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} that eliminates the target encoder (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Pretraining loss}: $\loss = \|\hat{\h}_{(t,t+\Delta t]} - \text{sg}(\h^*_{(t,t+\Delta t]})\|_1 + \lambda \cdot \text{var-reg}$, where sg denotes stop-gradient.

\subsection{Downstream: Freeze Encoder, Finetune Predictor}
\label{sec:downstream}

Given event labels, we freeze $\encoder$ and finetune $\predictor$ + a linear head:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Finetuning modes. Predictor-FT is our default.}
  \label{tab:finetune_modes}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcccr@{}}
    \toprule
    Mode                  & Encoder         & Predictor          & Head                 & Params tuned             \\
    \midrule
    Frozen probe          & frozen          & frozen             & LN + linear          & 513                      \\
    \textbf{Predictor-FT} & \textbf{frozen} & \textbf{finetuned} & \textbf{LN + linear} & \textbf{$\approx$\,198K} \\
    E2E                   & finetuned       & finetuned          & LN + linear          & $\approx$\,2.16M         \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predictor is run at sorted horizons $\mathcal{H} = \{{\Delta t}_1 < \cdots < {\Delta t}_K\}$. A shared linear head maps each predicted representation to a per-interval \emph{conditional hazard}:
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_k(t) \;=\; \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \hat{\h}_{(t, t+\Delta t_k]} + b\bigr) \;\in\; (0, 1),
  \label{eq:hazard}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_k(t) = P(\text{event in } (\Delta t_{k-1}, \Delta t_k] \mid T^* > \Delta t_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_{\leq t})$ and $T^*$ is the time-to-next-event. No distributional assumptions are required: each $\lambda_k$ is a free function of $\h_t$ via the predictor network. The event probability surface is the discrete-time survival CDF~\citep{lee2018deephit,ren2019drsa}:
\begin{equation}
  p(t, \Delta t_k) \;=\; 1 - \prod_{j=1}^{k} (1 - \lambda_j(t)).
  \label{eq:event_prob}
\end{equation}
The finetuning loss sums positive-weighted BCE over horizons:
\begin{equation}
  \loss_\text{FT} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w^+ \cdot \text{BCE}\bigl(p(t, \Delta t_k),\; y(t, \Delta t_k)\bigr),
  \label{eq:ft_loss}
\end{equation}
where $y(t, \Delta t) = \mathbb{1}[\text{event in } (t, t{+}\Delta t]]$ and $w^+ = N_{\text{neg}} / N_{\text{pos}}$. Two properties follow from the parameterization by construction:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity by construction.} Each factor $(1 - \lambda_j) \in (0, 1)$, so the survival product $\prod_{j \leq k}(1 - \lambda_j)$ is non-increasing in $k$, and $p(t, \Delta t_k) = 1 - \prod(\cdot)$ is non-decreasing. Zero violations by design.
  \item \textbf{Interval arithmetic.} $P(\text{event in } (t{+}a,\; t{+}b]) = p(t, b) - p(t, a)$ for $b > a$, since $p$ is a valid CDF.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:metrics}

The model outputs $p(t, \Delta t)$ (\cref{eq:event_prob}) for each observation time $t$ and prediction horizon $\Delta t$. This probability surface is the complete prediction; every metric we report is a readout of it. We compute exactly the metrics each baseline uses - RMSE for C-MAPSS~\citep{fan2024star}, PA-F1 for anomaly benchmarks~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,mtsjepa2026}, AUROC for clinical datasets - from the same stored surface, enabling direct comparison. As a cross-domain metric, we use \textbf{h-AUROC}: the mean of per-horizon AUROC values pooled over $(t, \Delta t)$ cells, which is threshold-free and robust to class imbalance. \Cref{tab:metric_projections} shows how standard metrics relate to the surface as lossy projections.

\paragraph{FAM vs.\ Chronos-2.} FAM wins 6 of 8 head-to-head comparisons at 100\% labels; on 5 of these the margin exceeds $+0.05$ h-AUROC. \paragraph{Label efficiency.} At 10\% labels, lifecycle datasets retain ${\geq}97\%$ of full-label h-AUROC. Streaming-anomaly datasets depend on entity coverage (see \cref{sec:label_efficiency}).

\paragraph{Legacy metrics.} C-MAPSS RMSE is derived from $\text{E}[\Delta t]$ of the stored probability surface; this CDF-based estimator differs from the point-prediction protocol of STAR~\citep{fan2024star}, so the gap reflects both protocol and SSL-vs-supervised differences. PA-F1 matches the cited baselines' protocol; see \cref{app:pa_f1} for inflation analysis.

\paragraph{Other foundation models.} Three additional foundation models - MOMENT-1-large (341M), TimesFM-1.0-200M, and Moirai-1.1-R-base (91M) - are evaluated with identical 198K-param head and protocol (\cref{app:moment_full,app:extra_baselines}). Against MOMENT (10 feasible datasets; SMD excluded at ${\approx}10.6$\,h extraction): FAM wins on degradation datasets; MOMENT wins MBA (MIMIC-III pretraining overlap) and FD002 (multi-condition turbofan); full results in \cref{app:moment_full}. Against TimesFM across 11 datasets: FAM wins 4 of 11; TimesFM wins on short-burst anomaly datasets (GECCO, BATADAL, SKAB, MBA, PSM, FD002, SMD) where large-corpus pretraining of frequent signal patterns is advantageous; FAM wins decisively on physical-degradation datasets (FD001 $+0.256$, FD003 $+0.238$, ETTm1 $+0.280$, SMAP $+0.093$). On SMD the margin is small ($+0.012$, std $0.019$ vs FAM $0.004$) indicating near-tie on multi-machine server data. Against Moirai (9 feasible datasets; SMAP and SMD infeasible due to $B{\times}C$ univariate patching, $N{\times}C{\geq}4.8$M sequences): FAM wins 6 of 9; Moirai wins FD002 ($+0.098$, multi-condition turbofan), SKAB ($+0.116$, periodic sensor patterns), and GECCO ($+0.003$, effectively tied with overlapping CIs). Full table in \cref{app:extra_baselines}.

\subsection{Label Efficiency}
\label{sec:label_efficiency}

\textbf{Two regimes by dataset type.} Lifecycle datasets retain ${\geq}97\%$ of full-label h-AUROC at 10\% labels (C-MAPSS, SMAP; \cref{tab:benchmark}): the frozen encoder already captures degradation dynamics from pretraining, so few labels suffice to steer the predictor. Streaming-anomaly datasets depend on entity coverage: SMD falls near chance at 10\% (only 3/28 entities retained); GECCO is excluded (zero positives in the truncated period).

\textbf{Sub-5\% label efficiency.} On C-MAPSS, $\mathbf{92\%}$ of full-label h-AUROC is retained with just 2 training engines out of 85 (\cref{tab:sub5pct}). The multi-fault subset (FD003) degrades faster (74\% at 2\%) due to its 4 distinct fault modes. This robustness is predicted by \cref{prop:retention}: pretraining establishes $I(H_t; E_{t+\Delta t})$ from unlabeled data; downstream learning only maps representations to event probabilities.

\textbf{Pred-FT vs.\ E2E at low labels.} Under the canonical architecture, pred-FT and E2E achieve equivalent AUPRC at all label budgets ($\Delta \leq 0.003$). The advantage of pred-FT is computational (${\sim}11{\times}$ fewer trainable parameters) rather than statistical; the gap visible under the legacy backbone (\cref{app:label_efficiency_full}) does not survive RevIN and cumulative targets.

\paragraph{Additional ablations.} Predictor-inference contribution, pretrained vs.\ random-init predictor, EMA vs.\ SIGReg pretraining, and architecture sensitivity are in \cref{app:additional_ablations}. Key findings: (i)~pretrained predictor weights carry at most $+0.003$ AUPRC over random-init ($p > 0.08$ on all datasets); (ii)~SIGReg-pred pretraining improves F1w by $+0.06$ and RMSE by $-3.2$ over EMA on C-MAPSS.

% 7. LIMITATIONS
% ============================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Per-dataset pretraining.} We pretrain a separate encoder per dataset. Cross-domain pretraining on a union of datasets is the natural next step.
  \item \textbf{C-MAPSS multi-fault regression.} AUPRC drops to $0.766$ on the multi-fault subset under the canonical $P{=}16$ architecture; the multi-fault degradation mode may need finer temporal resolution or more predictor capacity.
  \item \textbf{Chronos-2 parity on well-covered domains.} On C-MAPSS FD002 and GECCO, Chronos-2 matches or marginally outperforms FAM, suggesting its broad pretraining corpus covers some signal structures better than per-dataset JEPA.
  \item \textbf{Anomaly ``prediction'' vs detection.} On SMAP, the model detects ongoing anomaly segments rather than predicting onset with lead time. True early warning requires lead-time-aware evaluation.
  \item \textbf{MSL failure.} MSL (55 channels) falls below chance (h-AUROC $0.35$), likely because the high channel count dilutes per-channel degradation signals under channel-fusion tokenization. Sensor-as-token or channel-selection strategies may help.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive tokenization.} We fix $P{=}16$ following PatchTST; adapting $P$ to signal characteristics is an open direction.
\end{itemize}

% ============================================================
% 8. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

Foundation models forecast future values; practitioners care about events. FAM bridges this gap with two contributions. First, predictor finetuning: by freezing the encoder and tuning only a 198K-param predictor, FAM retains 92\% of full-label performance with just 2\% of labels on C-MAPSS, because the encoder already captures precursor dynamics from self-supervised pretraining. Our information-theoretic analysis formalises precisely when this holds. Second, one architecture across event types: a single 2.16M-param model handles degradation, anomaly, and clinical prediction across 11 datasets, winning 6 of 8 comparisons against foundation models at $56{\times}$ fewer parameters with identical downstream heads. The probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$, structured as a discrete-time survival CDF, unifies these tasks under one threshold-free evaluation. The encoder learns what changes; the predictor learns what matters.

\section{Horizon Interval Design}
\label{app:horizon_sensitivity}

FAM uses $K = 150$ densely-sampled horizon intervals $\Delta t \in \{1, 2, \ldots, 150\}$ during both pretraining and downstream evaluation. This design choice ensures the probability surface $p(t, \Delta t)$ captures fine-grained temporal structure. Foundation model baselines (Chr-2, MOMENT, TimesFM, Moirai) use $K = 7$ horizons $[1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 150]$ for C-MAPSS and $K = 8$ for anomaly datasets to avoid sequence-length bottlenecks in the frozen backbone. The h-AUROC evaluation uses whichever horizons are available, skipping degenerate horizons (event prevalence $< 0.001$ or $> 0.999$). A full horizon-count sweep is deferred to a future version.

\section{PA-F1 Inflation}
\label{app:pa_f1}

PA-F1 (Point-Adjusted F1) credits an entire anomaly segment as correctly predicted if any single timestep within it exceeds the threshold~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer}. This protocol inflates reported F1 dramatically when anomaly segments are long and prevalence is non-trivial: the TSAD-Eval study~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval} shows that a random detector can exceed $F_1 = 0.9$ PA on some datasets. We concretely demonstrate this by contrasting PA-F1 and non-PA F1 for FAM+Mahalanobis on our benchmarks: SMAP $0.793$ PA vs.\ $0.038$ non-PA, SMD $0.252$ PA vs.\ $0.144$ non-PA, PSM $0.813$ PA vs.\ $0.085$ non-PA. The gap in PA-F1 between FAM and MTS-JEPA remains large even under a random-baseline sanity check (a random-init encoder + Mahalanobis baseline achieves $0.604 \pm 0.007$ PA-F1 on SMAP; FAM is $0.79$), so the PA-F1 margin is not purely inflation - but any single PA-F1 number should be read in conjunction with the corresponding non-PA number.
