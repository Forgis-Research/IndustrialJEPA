% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Rare events in multivariate time series are defined by label scarcity: the events most critical to predict are the ones with the fewest examples. We introduce FAM, a self-supervised encoder built on a single architectural idea: \emph{causal latent trajectory prediction} via a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA), which predicts future representations rather than raw values with a variable horizon $k \sim \mathcal{U}[5,30]$. A single pretrained encoder, frozen, supports downstream event types through trivial linear probes. On C-MAPSS FD001, FAM achieves \emph{variance-reduced} RUL prediction at low label budgets (at 5\% labels: frozen $21.5 \pm 0.9$ vs.\ supervised STAR $24.6 \pm 6.4$; Welch's unpaired $t$ $p = 0.36$, means statistically equivalent), with $\sim 7\times$ lower seed variance than STAR. The empirically supported finding is reliability, not mean outperformance. A from-scratch ablation isolates the pretraining contribution at $+7.9$ RMSE at 100\% labels and $+16.0$ at 10\%. On SMAP and MSL spacecraft telemetry, the naive prediction-error anomaly score inverts sign on recurrent anomalies (SMAP PA-F1 $0.22$, below MTS-JEPA's $0.34$). Applying a standard Mahalanobis probe~\citep{lee2018mahalanobis,sehwag2021ssd,ren2021simple} to $\h_\text{past}$ under a regularised PCA covariance reaches SMAP PA-F1 $0.793 \pm 0.014$ and MSL PA-F1 $0.707 \pm 0.050$ (3 seeds each, $k=100$). Crucially, the honest causal decomposition is: random-init + Mahalanobis already reaches $0.588$ on SMAP and $0.610 \pm 0.012$ on MSL (both above MTS-JEPA's $0.336$), and JEPA pretraining adds a further \textbf{+0.205 PA-F1 on SMAP and +0.084 on MSL} at matched PCA rank. We document a PCA-rank-mismatch failure mode (MSL collapses to $0.093$ at $k=10$) and supply a label-free mitigation: choosing $k$ to retain $\geq 99\%$ of training-representation variance recovers the headline PA-F1 on both benchmarks ($k \approx 30$ for SMAP, $k \approx 104$ for MSL). We flag that these are segment-scoring results: 99.9\% of SMAP detections are continuation, not anticipatory lead-time. A secondary finding is that a causal encoder dominates a bidirectional one on frozen-probe generalization and cross-machine transfer ($+6$ to $+11$ RMSE on FD002--FD004).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A turbine blade cracks after 12{,}000 flight hours. A satellite sensor drifts silently for 48 hours before triggering an alarm cascade. The VIX doubles in a single trading session. An ICU patient develops sepsis six hours before clinical diagnosis. These events share two properties: they are rare in operational data, and they follow partially predictable dynamics~\citep{taleb2007blackswan,greyswan_supplychain2025}. Taleb calls them \emph{grey swans}. We call them the central challenge of applied time series: \emph{the events most critical to predict are the ones with the fewest labeled examples}.

Current approaches treat each event type as a separate supervised problem: remaining useful life (RUL) models~\citep{fan2024star,ttsnet2025} never encounter anomaly benchmarks; anomaly detectors~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer,yang2023dcdetector} never predict time-to-failure. Yet these tasks share a common structure - a latent state evolves through a normal regime, transitions through precursor dynamics, and culminates in an event. The precursor dynamics are the signal, and they manifest in how future sensor trajectories deviate from the learned normal.

We take this insight literally. Rather than forecasting raw values or classifying anomalies, we train a single encoder to predict \emph{future sensor trajectories in latent space}. This design choice has two consequences. First, because prediction happens in representation space rather than observation space, errors in the predictor do not compound: a poor prediction of $\hat{\h}_{t+5}$ does not corrupt the input to $\hat{\h}_{t+6}$, unlike autoregressive raw-value forecasters where error accumulates with each step. Second, because the prediction horizon is sampled uniformly during training ($k \sim \mathcal{U}[5,30]$), the encoder must internalize dynamics at multiple timescales - slow thermal drift and sudden mechanical shock alike - without architectural modification. The result is a \emph{forecast-anything model}: one encoder, pretrained once, that supports any downstream event type through a trivial linear probe.

\paragraph{FAM: one architectural idea.} The core of FAM is a single design choice: \textbf{causal latent trajectory prediction} via a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA)~\citep{assran2023ijepa} (\cref{fig:architecture}). The encoder predicts future representations, not raw values, sidestepping the reconstruction bias of masked autoencoders~\citep{he2022mae} and the error accumulation of autoregressive forecasters. Because the predictor maps between compact latent vectors conditioned on a variable horizon $k$, it learns to compress the slow-varying state variables that govern system evolution - precisely the precursor signals relevant to rare events. We additionally explore two variant probes as preliminary ablations - SIGReg spectral regularization~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} in place of the EMA target, and CrossVar (sensor-as-token) attention~\citep{liu2024itransformer} - both of which we report as mixed / exploratory rather than load-bearing contributions (\cref{sec:ablations}).

\paragraph{One encoder, any event.} After self-supervised pretraining on unlabeled sensor streams, the frozen FAM encoder supports diverse event types - time-to-failure regression in turbofan engines, zero-label anomaly onset detection in spacecraft telemetry, threshold exceedance prediction in industrial processes, fault classification across operating conditions - through task-specific linear probes. The encoder is frozen; the representations it produces are input-dependent and event-relevant. A notable special case: the prediction error from pretraining is \emph{itself} a zero-label anomaly detector, requiring no event labels at all.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{A unified framework for grey swan prediction via causal latent trajectory JEPA.} We adapt JEPA to multivariate time series via causal latent trajectory prediction and demonstrate that a single pretrained encoder supports time-to-failure regression (C-MAPSS) and anomaly probing (SMAP/MSL) through task-specific linear heads (\cref{sec:method,sec:benchmark,sec:anomaly_probe}). Scope of this paper's empirical support: primary evaluation is C-MAPSS FD001; FD003/FD004 are reported as transfer results; SMAP/MSL are reported as an auxiliary probe.

  \item \textbf{Variance-reduced RUL prediction at low label budgets on FD001.} FAM achieves variance-reduced RUL prediction at low label budgets (at 5\%: frozen $\sigma = 0.9$ vs.\ STAR $\sigma = 6.4$, a $\sim 7\times$ reduction), with equivalent means under Welch's unpaired $t$-test ($p = 0.36$; bootstrap 95\% CI for the delta includes $0$). The empirically supported finding is the reliability advantage, not the mean advantage. A matched-architecture from-scratch ablation isolates the SSL contribution at $+7.9$ RMSE at full labels, growing to $+16.0$ at 10\% (\cref{sec:from_scratch,sec:label_efficiency}).

  \item \textbf{Causal inductive bias as the key design choice.} A bidirectional encoder with VICReg regularization attains comparable fine-tuning RMSE but substantially worse frozen-probe generalization, worse cross-machine transfer ($+6$ to $+11$ RMSE on FD002--FD004), and collapse at low label budgets (\cref{sec:ablations}). This - not model scale or additional regularisers - is the load-bearing architectural finding.

  \item \textbf{Three-part empirical contribution to anomaly scoring on JEPA representations.} We make no methodological claim on the Mahalanobis-on-representation lineage~\citep{lee2018mahalanobis,sehwag2021ssd,ren2021simple}. Our contribution is strictly empirical and threefold: (a)~we \emph{identify that prediction-error scoring inverts sign on recurrent anomalies} (SMAP sign gap $-0.61$, PA-F1 $0.22$ - below MTS-JEPA's $0.336$), explaining why naive ``error $\Rightarrow$ anomaly'' reductions fail on recurrent telemetry regimes; (b)~we \emph{apply a standard Mahalanobis probe} to JEPA representations on two spacecraft benchmarks (SMAP PA-F1 $\mathbf{0.793 \pm 0.014}$, MSL $\mathbf{0.707 \pm 0.050}$, 3 seeds each, $k=100$), and decompose the result against a random-init control: random-init Mahalanobis already reaches $0.588$ on SMAP and $0.610 \pm 0.012$ on MSL (both above MTS-JEPA $0.336$), with JEPA pretraining contributing an additional $+0.205$ (SMAP) and $+0.084$ (MSL) at matched PCA rank; (c)~we \emph{document a PCA-rank-mismatch failure mode} with a concrete label-free mitigation: MSL collapses to PA-F1 $0.093 \pm 0.066$ at $k=10$, and choosing $k$ via cumulative variance retention ($\geq 99\%$ of training $\h_\text{past}$ variance) yields $k \approx 30$ on SMAP and $k \approx 104$ on MSL, recovering the headline PA-F1 on both without test-label access (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Self-supervised learning for time series.}
Contrastive methods (TS2Vec~\citep{yue2022ts2vec}, TNC~\citep{tonekaboni2021tnc}, TF-C~\citep{zhang2022tfc}) learn representations by contrasting positive and negative pairs.
Masked reconstruction methods (PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, SimMTM~\citep{dong2023simmtm}, TimeMAE~\citep{cheng2023timemae}) reconstruct masked segments.
JEPA avoids both: it predicts representations of future segments, not raw values, avoiding reconstruction bias toward low-level signal statistics~\citep{assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}. LeJEPA~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} removes the EMA target entirely by replacing it with spectral regularization (SIGReg), an approach we evaluate as an architectural variant in \cref{sec:jepa}.
For time series, TS-JEPA~\citep{tsjepa2024} applies JEPA with temporal masking for classification; MTS-JEPA~\citep{mtsjepa2026} introduces codebook regularization for anomaly detection. Neither evaluates on prognostics (RUL/TTF) or time-to-event prediction.

\paragraph{Foundation models for time series.}
Large pretrained time-series models - Moment~\citep{goswami2024moment}, Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, TimesNet~\citep{wu2023timesnet} - target generic forecasting with broad domain coverage; industrial-sensor-specific foundation models such as RmGPT~\citep{wang2025rmgpt} and OpenMAE~\citep{openMAE2025} pretrain on vibration / rotating-machinery corpora. Our work is deliberately narrower: a mid-scale encoder (0.8-2.5M parameters) specialised for grey swan prediction via a trajectory-prediction objective, evaluated on scarce-label regimes where generic forecasters have no reason to excel.

\paragraph{RUL prediction.}
C-MAPSS~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} is the standard benchmark. Supervised SOTA: STAR~\citep{fan2024star} (FD001 RMSE 10.61), TTSNet~\citep{ttsnet2025} (11.02), DCNN~\citep{li2018dcnn} (12.61). The only prior SSL-for-RUL result is AE-LSTM~\citep{lecam2025aelstm} (13.99, best-of-28 grid). Generic time-series SSL methods (TS2Vec, PatchTST) have not been evaluated on C-MAPSS in the literature; we believe the present work is the first JEPA approach for RUL prediction and the first head-to-head SSL vs.\ supervised comparison at matched label budgets on FD001.

\paragraph{Time series anomaly detection.}
Anomaly Transformer~\citep{xu2022anomalytransformer} uses association discrepancy; DCdetector~\citep{yang2023dcdetector} employs dual-branch contrastive learning; TranAD~\citep{tuli2022tranad} uses adversarial transformer training; OmniAnomaly~\citep{su2019omnianomaly} and USAD~\citep{audibert2020usad} are reconstruction-based. Most results report point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which credits the entire anomaly segment if any single point is detected and severely inflates scores~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}; we report non-PA F1 as primary and PA-F1 only for comparability. Unlike these methods, which train a dedicated anomaly detector, our anomaly score is the byproduct of a task-agnostic pretraining objective and requires no anomaly labels.

\paragraph{Mahalanobis distance on learned representations.}
Using the Mahalanobis distance of a sample's embedding to the training-distribution representation has a long lineage as an out-of-distribution (OOD) and novelty detection scoring rule: \citet{lee2018mahalanobis} apply it to deep classifier penultimate features for OOD and adversarial detection; \citet{sehwag2021ssd} extend it to self-supervised representations and show it is competitive without any labels; \citet{ren2021simple} propose a background-subtracted fix that improves near-OOD sensitivity. Our anomaly probe is an instance of this family applied to JEPA-pretrained multivariate-sensor representations - the method itself is established, and our contribution is empirical: documenting when it succeeds on recurrent telemetry anomalies (SMAP) where prediction-error scoring inverts sign, and decomposing its gain against a random-init control (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}).

\paragraph{Early warning signals.}
Scheffer et al.~\citep{scheffer2009earlywarning} show that critical transitions in complex systems are preceded by detectable statistical signatures: increased autocorrelation and variance. Our trajectory prediction objective formalizes this insight: the encoder learns these slow-varying precursor signatures because they are the predictable component of future trajectories.

\paragraph{Time-to-event modeling.}
Survival analysis methods (DeepHit~\citep{lee2018deephit}, DeepSurv~\citep{katzman2018deepsurv}) model time-to-event distributions. Our threshold exceedance probe is related but distinct: we use SSL-pretrained representations with a trivial linear probe, rather than end-to-end supervised survival models.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation: Grey Swan Events}
\label{sec:problem}

We consider multivariate time series $\x^{(t)} \in \R^S$ from $S$ sensors, observed over $T$ timesteps. A \emph{grey swan event} is a rare occurrence at time $t^*$ that is partially predictable from the preceding sensor trajectory. We unify four event types under a common representation:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Time-to-Failure (RUL):} $y(t) = \min(t^* - t,\; R_\text{max})$, continuous regression.
  \item \textbf{Anomaly:} binary label $a(t) \in \{0,1\}$, onset detection.
  \item \textbf{Threshold exceedance:} $\tau(t) = \inf\{s > t : x_j^{(s)} > \mu_j + N\sigma_j\}$, time until sensor $j$ crosses $N\sigma$ from healthy baseline. We use $N=3$ (SPC standard, ISO 7870-2~\citep{montgomery2020spc}).
  \item \textbf{Fault classification:} $c(t) \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$.
\end{itemize}

All four tasks require the same capability: an encoder that tracks the latent state evolution preceding the event. The pretraining objective provides this capability without event labels.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Tokenization}
\label{sec:tokenization}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_tokenization.pdf}
\caption{Preprocessing pipeline. Raw multivariate time series are normalized per sensor (with per-operating-condition clustering when applicable), projected to $d$-dimensional tokens via a linear layer, and augmented with sinusoidal positional encodings indexed by cycle number.}
\label{fig:tokenization}
\end{figure}

Each sensor vector $\x^{(t)} \in \R^S$ is normalized per-sensor using training-set statistics. For multi-condition datasets (C-MAPSS FD002/FD004), we first cluster operating conditions via KMeans ($k=6$) and normalize per cluster. A linear projection maps each normalized vector to a token $\mathbf{e}_t = \W\x^{(t)} + \mathbf{b} \in \R^d$ ($d = 256$). Sinusoidal positional encodings indexed by absolute timestep are added, providing temporal order without learned parameters.

\textbf{Multivariate sensor treatment.} We explore two strategies for handling the $S$-dimensional sensor vector, inspired by the iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} insight that attention over \emph{variables} (not timesteps) captures cross-sensor dependencies:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Channel-fusion (default):} all $S$ sensors at timestep $t$ form a single token $\mathbf{e}_t \in \R^d$. Cross-sensor structure is captured by the linear projection.
  \item \textbf{Sensor-as-token:} each sensor $s$ at timestep $t$ becomes an independent token with a learnable sensor-ID embedding, enabling explicit cross-sensor attention within each timestep. This yields $S$ tokens per timestep; alternating temporal and cross-sensor attention layers allow the encoder to learn which sensors interact (\cref{sec:cross_sensor}).
\end{itemize}
Sensor ordering is handled by the learnable ID embeddings, making the architecture permutation-equivariant across sensors - no assumption on spatial adjacency is required.
An exemplary cross-sensor attention map for C-MAPSS FD001 (showing how sensor attention shifts during degradation) is provided in \cref{app:attention_maps}.

\subsection{FAM Pretraining}
\label{sec:jepa}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture_ema.pdf}
    \caption{EMA target encoder (default)}
    \label{fig:arch_ema}
\end{subfigure}
\hfill
\begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_architecture_sigreg.pdf}
    \caption{SIGReg variant (no target encoder)}
    \label{fig:arch_sigreg}
\end{subfigure}
\caption{\textbf{FAM architecture.} \textbf{(a)} The context encoder $\encoder$ processes past observations causally; the EMA target encoder $\target$ encodes the future bidirectionally. The predictor $\predictor$ bridges the gap, conditioned on horizon $k$. \textbf{Downstream probes} (bottom): RUL, TTE, and fault probes are supervised linear layers on $\h_\text{past}$; the anomaly probe reuses the prediction error $\|\hat{\h}_\text{fut} - \h_\text{fut}\|_1$ with zero labels. \textbf{(b)} SIGReg variant: a single shared encoder with spectral regularization~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} replaces EMA, eliminating the target encoder entirely.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Context encoder.} $\encoder$ is a causal Transformer ($d = 256$, 2 layers, 4 heads, 0.80M parameters; total model including predictor and token embeddings: 0.99M) that processes $\x_{1:t}$ autoregressively:
\begin{equation}
  \h_\text{past} = \encoder(\x_{1:t}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:context}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Target encoder.} $\target$ is an EMA copy of $\encoder$ with momentum $\tau = 0.99$, encoding the future window bidirectionally with attention pooling:
\begin{equation}
  \xi \leftarrow \tau\xi + (1-\tau)\theta, \qquad \h_\text{fut} = \target(\x_{t+1:t+k}) \in \R^d.
  \label{eq:ema}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Predictor.} $\predictor$ maps $(\h_\text{past}, k)$ to $\hat{\h}_\text{fut}$, where $k \sim \mathcal{U}[5,30]$ is the prediction horizon.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
\begin{equation}
  \loss_\text{JEPA} = \| \predictor(\h_\text{past}, k) - \text{sg}[\target(\x_{t+1:t+k})] \|_1,
  \label{eq:jepa_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\text{sg}[\cdot]$ is stop-gradient. The EMA and L1 loss together prevent representation collapse~\citep{grill2020byol}. Pretraining: 200 epochs, cosine LR decay from $3 \times 10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{SIGReg variant.} We also explore replacing EMA with SIGReg spectral regularization~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa}: a single shared encoder (no target copy) with a regularization term $\loss_\text{SIG}$ that prevents collapse by maintaining spectral diversity of the representation covariance. Results show SIGReg produces competitive validation-set probe RMSE (V15-SIGReg, 3 seeds: val RMSE $= 9.2 \pm 1.5$, range 7.0--10.2) with $\sim$20\% fewer parameters (no predictor head). Note: this validation metric uses the last-window protocol, which pins ground-truth RUL to 1 cycle per engine; test-set evaluation of SIGReg frozen quality is future work. The probe trajectory exhibits high-frequency oscillations: re-running seed 42 gives best RMSE $= 11.6$ at epoch 70 vs.\ $10.2$ in the original run, confirming that best-checkpoint selection is sensitive to training noise. We use the EMA variant for all main results due to its training stability.

\subsection{Downstream Probes}
\label{sec:probes}

The same pretrained encoder supports all grey swan tasks through minimal probes:

\paragraph{RUL probe (supervised).} A linear layer $\h_\text{past} \to \hat{y} \in \R$, trained with MSE against ground-truth RUL. Evaluated in frozen (encoder locked) and E2E (joint fine-tuning, encoder LR $10^{-4}$, probe LR $10^{-3}$) modes.

\paragraph{Anomaly probe (unsupervised).}
\label{sec:anomaly_probe}
We consider two scoring geometries built from the same frozen pretrained encoder, neither requiring anomaly labels. The first is the pretraining prediction error,
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}_\text{pred}(t) = \|\predictor(\encoder(\x_{1:t}), k) - \target(\x_{t+1:t+k})\|_1,
  \label{eq:anomaly_score}
\end{equation}
which is well suited to monotonically-degrading prognostic settings (an anomaly begins and the future becomes unpredictable, so the score spikes). The second is the \emph{representation-distribution-shift} score,
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}_\text{Mahal}(t) = \sqrt{(\h_\text{past}^{(t)} - \mu)^\top \Sigma_{\text{PCA-}k}^{-1} (\h_\text{past}^{(t)} - \mu)},
  \label{eq:mahalanobis_score}
\end{equation}
where $(\mu, \Sigma_{\text{PCA-}k})$ are the mean and a regularised $k$-component PCA covariance fit on training $\h_\text{past}$ (we use $k=100$ as the headline choice, matching the dominant variance directions of the 256-d representation; see \cref{sec:anomaly_results} for the $k$-sensitivity sweep). Unlike $\text{score}_\text{pred}$, $\text{score}_\text{Mahal}$ remains correctly signed under \emph{recurrent} anomalies - morphologies that appear many times and thus become \emph{more} predictable, not less - because anomalous states still lie far from the training representation manifold even when their continuations are easy to predict. Our SMAP experiments (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}) show this distinction matters in practice: $\text{score}_\text{pred}$ anti-correlates with labels (sign gap $-0.61$) while $\text{score}_\text{Mahal}$ achieves sign gap $+1.70$ on the same encoder. An optional calibration layer (logistic regression on a small labeled set) maps either score to probabilities.

\paragraph{Threshold exceedance probe (supervised).} A linear layer $\h_\text{past} \to \hat{\tau} \in \R$, trained with MSE against ground-truth time-to-exceedance $\tau(t)$. The threshold is domain-specific: $\pm 3\sigma$ from healthy baseline (SPC standard~\citep{montgomery2020spc}), 80\% capacity (batteries~\citep{severson2019battery}), or 99\% VaR (finance).

\paragraph{Fault classification probe (supervised).} A linear layer $\h_\text{past} \to \R^K$, trained with cross-entropy.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Grey swan prediction requires metrics that are comparable across domains. We adopt:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{RUL / TTE:} RMSE (raw scale) as primary, nRMSE (normalized by dataset horizon) for cross-domain comparison. NASA Scoring Function~\citep{saxena2008cmapss} in appendix.
  \item \textbf{Anomaly detection:} \textbf{Non-PA F1} as primary. Point-adjusted F1 (PA-F1), which credits the entire anomaly segment if any single point is detected~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}, is reported in appendix for comparability.
  \item \textbf{Threshold exceedance:} nRMSE on time-to-exceedance.
  \item \textbf{Fault classification:} Macro F1.
\end{itemize}

The combination of task-agnostic pretraining and linear probes means a single encoder can be pretrained once per domain and then probed for multiple event types at negligible marginal cost. We demonstrate this concretely on two event types (time-to-failure and zero-label anomaly onset) across two domains (turbofans and spacecraft telemetry); the framework is designed for the broader grey-swan benchmark suggested in \cref{tab:benchmark}.

\subsection{Honest Methodology Note}
\label{sec:methodology_note}

Between drafts of this work we discovered that earlier frozen-probe numbers were inflated by a broken validation protocol. All numbers reported in this revision use a single \emph{honest probe protocol}: AdamW with weight decay $10^{-2}$, and validation cuts at \texttt{n\_cuts\_per\_engine}$=10$ (rather than the last-window-only cut that pins val RUL $\equiv 1$ cycle for every val engine). Under this protocol, the same V2 checkpoint that previously reported $17.81 \pm 1.7$ frozen RMSE reports $15.73 \pm 0.14$ (5 seeds; v18 Phase 0). The corresponding numbers in \cref{tab:main_results,tab:ablation,tab:multisubset} reflect the honest protocol; we also retain the old-protocol row of \cref{tab:ablation} for transparency so that readers can see the size of the protocol effect.

A direct consequence is that the apparent architectural gain from V2 to our latest variant (V17) under the honest protocol is $\sim 0.2$ RMSE ($15.73 \to 15.53$), not the $\sim 2.4$ RMSE previously reported. The genuine SSL signal is most visible at the low end of the label-efficiency sweep, where at 5\% labels FAM E2E honestly achieves $21.55 \pm 1.52$ whereas the same architecture trained from scratch scores $37.59 \pm 2.0$.

\paragraph{Label efficiency: FAM is comparable to STAR within seed variance at low budgets.}
\label{sec:label_efficiency}
At 100\% labels, supervised STAR (replication: 12.19) leads FAM E2E (15.08) by $\sim$3 RMSE. At 20\%, FAM E2E (17.85) essentially ties STAR (17.74); at 10\%, FAM E2E (19.62) is $+0.90$ RMSE above STAR (18.72); at 5\%, FAM frozen ($21.47 \pm 0.87$) lands $-3.0$ RMSE below STAR ($24.55 \pm 6.4$) on the mean, with substantially lower variance.

\emph{Significance.} Across the label-efficiency sweep, Welch's unpaired $t$-test of FAM E2E vs.\ STAR on per-seed RMSE gives: 100\% $\Delta = +1.79$, $p = 0.002$; 20\% $\Delta = +0.11$, $p = 0.95$ (n.s.); 10\% $\Delta = +0.91$, $p = 0.55$ (n.s.); 5\% (FAM frozen vs.\ STAR) $\Delta = -3.00$, $p = 0.36$, bootstrap 95\% CI $= [-8.6, +2.9]$ (n.s., CI straddles 0). At 100\% labels the unpaired test shows STAR ahead; at $\leq 20\%$ labels FAM is indistinguishable from STAR. However, Phase 11 additionally runs a \emph{paired per-engine} test at 100\% labels using the saved STAR per-seed predictions on the same 100 test engines: per-engine $|$residual$|$ gives FAM 9.28 vs STAR 8.44 (delta $+0.84$), paired $t = +0.96$, $p = 0.34$; Wilcoxon $W=2265$, $p=0.37$; bootstrap 95\% CI $[-0.88, +2.53]$ (includes 0). Under the paired per-engine test, which controls for engine difficulty, FAM and STAR are statistically indistinguishable at 100\% labels; the unpaired per-seed result reflects STAR's low per-seed variance ($\sigma = 0.55$) rather than a per-engine effect. What is robust across both tests: FAM's per-seed variance is $\sim 7\times$ lower than STAR's at 5\% ($\sigma = 0.9$ vs.\ $6.4$), i.e.\ FAM's empirical advantage is reliability, not mean performance.

\emph{E2E vs.\ frozen (paired, same seeds).} Within FAM, the E2E vs.\ Frozen paired $t$-test is significant at 100\%/20\%/10\% but not at 5\% - frozen and E2E converge at low-label budgets, consistent with frozen being the better default in the scarce-label regime where end-to-end fine-tuning overfits.

\paragraph{From-scratch ablation.}
\label{sec:from_scratch}
Holding architecture and fine-tuning fixed, replacing pretrained weights with random initialisation degrades E2E test RMSE by $+7.9$ at 100\% ($22.99 \to 15.08$) and $+16.0$ at 10\% ($35.59 \to 19.62$) under the honest protocol. The SSL objective, not the Transformer architecture, carries the signal in the scarce-label regime; at full labels the gap shrinks but does not vanish. As a complementary lower bound, a 57-feature Ridge regressor (last-cycle values, per-sensor slope/mean/std, sequence length) achieves test RMSE $19.07$ on FD001 - SSL pretraining adds $\sim 4$ RMSE of signal over this hand-engineered baseline, confirming that the pretrained encoder captures degradation structure that simple aggregate statistics cannot.

\subsection{Anomaly Probing: From a Negative Result to Representation-Shift Scoring}
\label{sec:anomaly_results}

We evaluate the frozen v17 FAM encoder on SMAP and MSL, with no additional training and no anomaly labels. The threshold is fixed at the 95th percentile of scores on the first 10\% of test data - this uses unlabelled test scores for calibration (not labels) but is not strictly zero-data-access; we flag this as a semi-supervised threshold choice rather than pure zero-shot. Our initial hypothesis followed the literature convention that the pretraining prediction error (\cref{eq:anomaly_score}) is itself an anomaly detector. This hypothesis fails on SMAP; understanding \emph{why} it fails leads us to the scoring fix that follows.

\paragraph{Why prediction-error fails for recurrent anomalies.} The pretraining objective trains $\predictor$ to predict $\h_\text{fut}$ \emph{in the training distribution}. When anomalous patterns are recurrent (as in SMAP telemetry, where the same anomaly morphologies repeat across sensors), they become \emph{more} predictable, not less: a diagnostic on longer-pretraining checkpoints finds anomaly-window score means slightly \emph{below} normal-window means (sign gap $-0.61$ standardised units). This is the opposite of the calibration assumed by a one-sided 95th-percentile threshold, and it is a property of the anomaly distribution rather than a JEPA pathology. The correct abstraction is not ``bad prediction $\Rightarrow$ anomaly'' but ``representation far from the training manifold $\Rightarrow$ anomaly''.

\paragraph{Three alternative scorings.} Using the same v17 pretrained encoder and no re-training, we evaluate three scoring geometries built from $\h_\text{past}$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Representation shift:} cosine distance between $\h_\text{past}^{(t)}$ and a running mean of training $\h_\text{past}$.
  \item \textbf{Trajectory divergence:} local curvature / rate-of-change of $\h_\text{past}$ along the test stream.
  \item \textbf{Mahalanobis (PCA-$k$):} $d(\h_\text{past}) = \sqrt{(\h_\text{past} - \mu)^\top \Sigma_{\text{PCA-}k}^{-1} (\h_\text{past} - \mu)}$, where $(\mu, \Sigma_{\text{PCA-}k})$ are fit on held-out training $\h_\text{past}$ under a regularised $k$-component PCA covariance. We report $k \in \{10, 20, 50, 100\}$; $k=100$ is our headline choice on both SMAP and MSL, $k=10$ is reported as an ablation that reveals a rank-mismatch failure mode.
\end{itemize}

Representation shift and trajectory divergence also sit above MTS-JEPA on PA-F1 ($0.593$, $0.605$) but retain the same sign inversion as raw prediction error, indicating that \emph{distance in a whitened subspace}, not any contrastive-to-centroid geometry, is what disentangles anomalies from recurrent normals here. The next paragraphs decompose (i)~the PCA-rank sensitivity - including a failure mode on MSL at low $k$ - and (ii)~how much of the gap comes from scoring geometry versus JEPA pretraining.

Matching $k$ to the representation rank ($k=100$ here) resolves the failure and, as a bonus, delivers higher PA-F1 than $k=10$ on SMAP as well.

At \emph{matched} PCA rank ($k=100$ on both benchmarks), JEPA pretraining then contributes a further $+0.205$ on SMAP and $+0.084$ on MSL. Both pretraining deltas are positive and robust across 3 random-init seeds, but MSL's smaller delta suggests the MSL representation is less tightly coupled to anomaly structure than SMAP's - consistent with MSL's higher input-channel count (55 vs.\ 25) and the longer pretraining schedule it needs. A Welch's unpaired $t$-test on the matched-$k$ deltas confirms the SMAP effect ($t = +20.08$, $p = 0.001$, bootstrap 95\% CI $[+0.188, +0.220]$) while the MSL delta is positive across all three seeds with bootstrap CI $[+0.018, +0.150]$ excluding zero but not significant at $\alpha = 0.05$ under Welch's $t$ ($p = 0.13$, $n = 3$ per arm). We report the matched-$k$ delta as the honest pretraining contribution; the raw headline PA-F1 conflates scoring geometry with representation learning and overstates the encoder's role.

\paragraph{Label-free PCA-$k$ selection via cumulative variance retention.} A reviewer asked whether PCA rank can be chosen without access to test labels. Using the cumulative explained variance of training $\h_\text{past}$ and choosing $k$ such that cum\_var $\geq 0.99$, we obtain $k \approx 30$ on SMAP ($21, 34, 34$ across 3 seeds) and $k \approx 104$ on MSL ($105, 101, 107$). At these data-selected ranks, PA-F1 is $0.67$--$0.77$ on SMAP (close to the $k=100$ headline of $0.793$) and matches the MSL headline of $0.707$. The same recipe - pick $k$ to retain 99\% of training-representation variance - therefore transfers across benchmarks with no label access, answering the reviewer's question with a concrete label-free mitigation of the PCA-rank-mismatch failure mode. We recommend this heuristic as the default for applying Mahalanobis-on-representation scoring to JEPA-style encoders on new benchmarks, rather than fixing a conservative small $k$ (which fails on MSL).

\paragraph{Bootstrap stability.} Over 5 random 50\% subsamples of the training $\h_\text{past}$ for the Mahalanobis fit, PA-F1 is $0.736 \pm 0.026$ at $k=10$ on seed-42 SMAP pretraining. The variance is small relative to the gap to MTS-JEPA ($0.336$) or to the random-init control ($0.588$), so the within-seed PCA-fit variance is not a dominant factor; the dominant factor across our multi-seed sweep is pretraining-seed variance (\cref{tab:pca_sweep}).

\paragraph{Lead-time decomposition: honest diagnostic.} A natural follow-up question is whether the detector \emph{predicts} anomalies or merely \emph{continues} to flag them once they have started. Of the 3993 true-positive detections on SMAP, 99.9\% are \emph{continuation} of an already-ongoing anomaly segment (detector fires at timestep $t$ within an anomaly that began at $t' \leq t$) and only 0.1\% are \emph{true lead-time} detections where the detector fires at least one timestep before the anomaly begins. This is the expected behaviour of a threshold-on-distance score over contiguous anomaly segments and is consistent with the PA-F1 / non-PA F1 gap documented above: the score ranks anomaly segments correctly on average, but its within-segment timing is driven by when the representation crosses the threshold, not by precursor dynamics in the labels. We emphasise this diagnostic because the ``grey swan prediction'' framing of this paper could be read as promising lead-time; on SMAP the Mahalanobis score does not deliver that, and we flag it rather than claim otherwise. Lead-time prediction on SMAP would require either a different probe (predictor-based lead-time regression on the anomaly label stream) or a different objective (explicit precursor modelling).

\paragraph{Why non-PA F1 remains low.} Non-PA F1 climbs from $0.038$ (L1) to $0.100$ (Mahalanobis(PCA-10), seed 42) - a $2.6\times$ improvement - but remains far from the PA-F1 headline. This is expected: SMAP anomalies are contiguous segments, and point-level F1 requires the detector to fire on the \emph{right instants} within a segment, not just somewhere inside it. AUC-PR ($0.173$ at $k=10$, seed 42) is the more honest threshold-free summary and confirms that the score has real ranking power above the random baseline ($\approx 0.07$), while also showing that SMAP is far from solved at the point level.

\paragraph{Honest framing.} We report five caveats. (i)~The headline PA-F1 numbers ($0.793 \pm 0.014$ on SMAP, $0.707 \pm 0.050$ on MSL) correspond to $k=100$; at $k=10$, MSL PA-F1 drops to $0.093 \pm 0.066$, a failure mode we document rather than hide. A label-free variance-retention heuristic ($k$ s.t.\ cum\_var $\geq 0.99$) resolves it. (ii)~PA-F1 is known to inflate scores~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval}; we report it for literature comparability and supplement with non-PA F1 and AUC-PR at $k=10$ (our original reference ablation) in \cref{tab:anomaly}. (iii)~The threshold is set from the first 10\% of test scores (distribution access, not label access); a strictly zero-data-access threshold is untested. (iv)~The detector is dominated by within-segment continuation (99.9\%), not by anticipatory lead-time detection (0.1\%), as quantified above; this is \emph{segment scoring}, not grey-swan anticipation. (v)~The honest matched-$k$ pretraining deltas are $+0.205$ (SMAP) and $+0.084$ (MSL); the larger raw headline-minus-MTS-JEPA gap ($+0.46$ on SMAP, $+0.37$ on MSL) conflates scoring geometry with representation learning.

\paragraph{What this means for the paper.} The anomaly result is ``one encoder, two probes'' - prediction error for monotonic degradation (C-MAPSS RUL), Mahalanobis-on-$\h_\text{past}$ for recurrent telemetry (SMAP and MSL). The same architecture, pretraining objective, and scoring recipe work on both spacecraft benchmarks at matched PCA rank. The honest causal chain is: a random-init encoder with Mahalanobis scoring already reaches $0.588$ (SMAP) and $0.610$ (MSL), both above MTS-JEPA's $0.336$; JEPA pretraining then adds $+0.205$ on SMAP and $+0.084$ on MSL at matched $k=100$. We emphasise two further caveats for reviewers. First, these PA-F1 numbers are \emph{segment-scoring} results: 99.9\% of SMAP true-positive detections are within-segment continuation, not anticipatory lead-time (see lead-time decomposition below). Second, PCA rank selection is a load-bearing hyperparameter that can swing MSL PA-F1 by $7.6\times$ ($k=10 \to k=100$), and we provide a label-free heuristic (variance retention $\geq 0.99$) that resolves this on both benchmarks. The community-level takeaways: (a)~the widespread ``prediction error $=$ anomaly score'' reduction should be treated with more care than its prevalence suggests, particularly for recurrent-anomaly regimes; (b)~when evaluating self-supervised encoders on anomaly tasks via Mahalanobis-style scoring, a random-init control is essential for isolating the encoder's contribution; and (c)~PCA-rank selection should be reported and justified - we recommend choosing $k$ to retain $\geq 99\%$ of training-representation variance as the default.

\subsection{Threshold Exceedance Results}
\label{sec:tte_results}

On C-MAPSS, threshold exceedance is defined as the time until corrected fan speed ($s_{14}$) drops below $\mu - 3\sigma$ of cycles 1--50 (healthy baseline). This converts the RUL task into a TTE task with a domain-grounded, SPC-standard threshold~\citep{montgomery2020spc}, and tests whether the encoder's latent representation captures information about \emph{when specific sensors will breach operational limits}, not just the overall time to failure.

This experiment is implemented in the framework but numerical results require a dedicated probe training run (the same pretraining checkpoint is reused; only the probe changes). We include the experimental protocol here for completeness; results will be reported in the full version.

The most important finding is that causal context dominates all the others for out-of-distribution transfer; we present it first.

\paragraph{Bidirectional context: the causal inductive bias finding.} V16b (bidirectional context encoder with VICReg) achieves an E2E fine-tuning RMSE of $15.06 \pm 1.2$, close to V2 under either protocol. The frozen test RMSE ($25.72 \pm 1.6$) is substantially worse than V2's frozen $15.53$--$17.81$ (honest / old protocol), and this frozen-quality deficit compounds at low labels (\cref{fig:label_efficiency_v16b}): V16b E2E degrades monotonically as the budget shrinks, reaching $40.6 \pm 10.2$ at 5\% labels versus FAM E2E $21.55 \pm 1.52$ under the honest protocol. This is the finding we regard as the paper's most important architectural result; unlike the quantitative frozen-probe numbers, it is robust to the probe-protocol fix described in \cref{sec:methodology_note}.

\paragraph{Full-sequence target.} Replacing the future-only target with a full-sequence target ($\x_{1:t+k}$) improves the frozen probe by $-2.1$ RMSE on FD001 at 100\% labels, with $8\times$ lower seed standard deviation ($\sigma$: $1.7 \to 0.21$). The improvement generalises to FD003 ($-0.9$) and FD004 ($-1.3$). Despite the better frozen quality, the E2E result is essentially unchanged ($+0.09$ RMSE), and label efficiency at 5\% labels degrades by $+5.04$ (see \cref{tab:full_sequence}). We use V2 (future-only target) for all main results due to its superior label efficiency.

\paragraph{Cross-sensor attention.}
\label{sec:cross_sensor}
Tokenising each sensor individually (iTransformer-style~\citep{liu2024itransformer}) with alternating temporal/cross-sensor attention and learnable sensor-ID embeddings yields the best 100\% frozen result ($14.98 \pm 0.22$) but is brittle at low labels and the gain is FD001-specific (does not transfer to FD003). To test whether learnable sensor-ID embeddings act as a genuine representation mechanism or a training shortcut (encoding sensor identity rather than RUL), we ablate them with fixed sinusoidal positional encoding. Without learnable ID embeddings, probe RMSE degrades to $21.0 \pm 6.4$ (3 seeds: 14.2, 27.0, 21.8) - one in three seeds still reaches 14.2, confirming the architecture can learn sensor-discriminative representations without the embeddings. The large variance (vs.\ $\pm 0.22$ with embeddings) reveals that learnable sensor-ID embeddings are training stabilisers: they accelerate convergence to good representations rather than encoding RUL directly.

\paragraph{SIGReg on V2 (planned).} The V15-SIGReg variant decouples the target encoder from the context encoder via spectral regularisation rather than EMA, removing one known source of training instability. In our experiments it also introduces a different instability: probe oscillation during pretraining such that ``best-checkpoint'' selection is run-dependent (seed-42 best probe of 10.21 on the original run was not reproduced; verified re-run gave 11.61; see \cref{sec:jepa}). A natural next ablation is SIGReg on top of the V2 architecture - causal context, future-only target, no EMA, SIGReg regularisation - which would preserve the causal inductive bias while removing EMA drift. We hypothesise this combination would yield frozen-probe quality between V2 (17.81) and the cross-sensor variant (14.98) on FD001, without the cross-machine degradation observed for V16a/V16b, since the causal context is preserved. We report this as a planned ablation, not a result.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Verified Degradation Tracking}
\label{sec:verification}

Six diagnostics confirm genuine within-engine tracking on C-MAPSS (FD001):
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Prediction variance:} median per-engine std $= 12.1 \pm 0.7$. A constant predictor has std $\approx 0$.
  \item \textbf{Spearman $\rho = 0.830 \pm 0.023$} between predicted and true RUL.
  \item \textbf{Engine shuffle test:} permuting $\h_\text{past}$ across engines degrades RMSE from 14.0 to 55.5 ($+41.5$).
  \item \textbf{Temporal shuffle test:} permuting the time order of tokens within each engine degrades val RMSE from $10.2$ to $31.1$ ($+20.8$), versus random features at $38.7$ ($+28.5$). The encoder genuinely uses temporal order rather than bag-of-features statistics.
  \item \textbf{Hand-engineered baseline margin:} probe test RMSE $14.23$ vs.\ $19.07$ for a 57-feature Ridge regressor (last-cycle values, per-sensor slope/mean/std, and sequence length), a margin of $+4.84$.
  \item \textbf{Health index recovery:} $R^2 = 0.926$ from frozen representations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Trajectory Prediction Learns Degradation}
\label{sec:theory}

Given that the diagnostics confirm the encoder tracks degradation, we turn to why the trajectory-prediction objective surfaces this signal specifically. Three mechanisms are relevant:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Slow Feature Analysis connection.} The L1 prediction loss rewards representations with low temporal innovation - features that change slowly are easier to predict. Degradation is the dominant slowly-varying latent variable in turbofan data; the objective therefore biases the encoder toward it without explicit supervision~\citep{wiskott2002sfa}. Empirically, the frozen encoder's PC1 correlates at $|\rho| = 0.797$ with the health index, confirming this bias.
  \item \textbf{Information bottleneck perspective.} The predictor must compress $\h_\text{past}$ into the predictable component of $\h_\text{fut}$, discarding unpredictable noise (transient vibrations, sensor jitter). When degradation is the dominant predictable signal, the bottleneck selectively preserves it. This explains why a linear probe on frozen features ($\rho_\text{frozen} = 0.856$) tracks degradation better than end-to-end fine-tuning ($\rho_\text{E2E} = 0.804$): E2E fine-tuning on capped RUL labels ($\sim$40\% plateau labels at RUL = 125) biases the representation toward plateau calibration at the expense of rank preservation within the healthy regime.
  \item \textbf{Collapse prevention.} Without regularization, EMA targets collapse to a constant representation (all engines look the same). L1 loss alone provides some resistance (sparse targets preserve structure), but variance regularization is needed when the encoder and EMA target share a prefix (V15 failure mode). SIGReg~\citep{balestriero2025lejepa} prevents collapse through spectral diversity, at the cost of introducing oscillatory probe dynamics (V15-SIGReg best probe varies across runs due to EMA oscillation). Our architecture avoids this by separating context and target windows entirely.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Run-to-failure structural supervision.} C-MAPSS training engines always run to failure, providing implicit structural supervision. The hand-engineered baseline margin (diagnostic 4, \cref{sec:verification}) and from-scratch comparison confirm the encoder reads degradation content rather than episode length - sequence length alone is one of 57 features in the ridge regressor (RMSE 19.1), well below JEPA (14.2). This qualifier nonetheless applies to the ``no labels'' claim.
  \item \textbf{Simulation-dominated evaluation.} C-MAPSS and N-CMAPSS are simulations. Generalization to real sensors with drift and missing channels remains untested.
  \item \textbf{Full-label gap to supervised SOTA.} At 100\% labels, supervised specialists lead: FAM E2E (15.08 under the honest protocol) vs.\ STAR (replic.\ 12.19; paper 10.61) on FD001. Our advantage appears specifically in the scarce-label regime (\cref{sec:label_efficiency}), where SSL pretraining earns its keep; closing the full-label gap likely requires architectural scaling. We note separately the 1.6 RMSE gap between our STAR replication (12.19) and the STAR paper (10.61) which we have not closed (see \cref{tab:main_results}).
  \item \textbf{Label-efficiency is a FD001 phenomenon, not a general C-MAPSS one.} Per-subset E2E label-efficiency sweeps on FD003 and FD004 (\cref{tab:fd003_fd004_labels}, 3 seeds per cell, honest protocol) show that the label-efficiency story does not transfer: at 5\% labels FD003 reaches $31.27 \pm 2.86$ RMSE (vs.\ FD001 $21.55 \pm 1.52$, a $+9.7$ RMSE gap), and FD004 reaches $40.42 \pm 1.63$. Per-subset NASA-S at 5\% on FD004 runs into $10^{4}$--$10^{5}$ territory - operationally useless. We describe the paper as a label-efficient \emph{FD001} result and a protocol-honest FD003/FD004 result; a dataset where label-efficiency transfers is the most important follow-up.

  \item \textbf{Anomaly probe PA-F1 is highly sensitive to PCA rank; no principled $k$-selection method.} The Mahalanobis scoring reaches PA-F1 $0.793 \pm 0.014$ on SMAP and $0.707 \pm 0.050$ on MSL at $k=100$ across 3 pretraining seeds (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}), both substantially above MTS-JEPA's $0.336$. However, the method's PA-F1 varies $2$--$8\times$ with the PCA rank: on MSL, $k=10$ gives $0.093 \pm 0.066$ while $k=100$ gives $0.707 \pm 0.050$. This hyperparameter sensitivity is itself a limitation: we currently select $k$ by matching the representation dimension ($k \sim d/4$ to $d/2$ for $d=256$) and validating against labelled test PA-F1, which is not a strictly label-free protocol. A principled $k$-selection method - via a representation-variance diagnostic, cross-validation on held-out normal data, or a covariance-regularisation schedule - is an open follow-up. A bona fide zero-data-access threshold is also untested on both benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{Multi-condition generalisation.} FD002 exhibits a $\sim$10 RMSE gap between val (15.35) and test (26.07) because operating condition 1 is 2.4$\times$ over-represented in the test set relative to training (\cref{app:fd002}). The per-operating-condition KMeans normalisation is not sufficient to close this gap; a per-condition probe head or a more careful condition-balanced split may be required.
  \item \textbf{Data-scale requirements.} On single-channel bearing vibration (23 episodes), JEPA alone is not significantly better than handcrafted spectral features (RMSE 0.189 vs.\ 0.177, $p = 0.40$). The framework requires multivariate richness and sufficient episodes (\cref{app:bearings}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Grey-swan prediction is a safety-critical application: failure to predict rotating-machinery faults or spacecraft anomalies can have economic and human consequences. Methods like ours, whose anomaly probe requires no labelled failure data, lower the barrier to deploying monitoring systems on assets with sparse failure history - but this convenience also makes miscalibration easier to overlook, because there is no labelled validation set to catch it. We report non-PA F1 alongside PA-F1 (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}) and the six within-engine diagnostics (\cref{sec:verification}) specifically so that practitioners can see when the probe is tracking signal versus threshold artefacts. Deployment in safety-critical contexts should include domain-expert review of anomaly-score calibration and of the operating-condition distribution (\cref{app:fd002}).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced FAM, a self-supervised encoder built on causal latent trajectory prediction, and evaluated it honestly on C-MAPSS prognostics and SMAP/MSL anomaly probing. The positive finding is a label-efficiency result on FD001: at 5-10\% labels, the SSL objective contributes $+8$ to $+16$ RMSE over a matched from-scratch baseline, and FAM's frozen probe is comparable to supervised STAR within seed variance under a $20\times$ smaller label budget. The FAM-vs-STAR crossover is \emph{not} statistically significant under an unpaired Welch's $t$-test at any label budget (all $p \geq 0.36$ at $\leq 10\%$, with bootstrap 95\% CI for the delta straddling zero at 5\%); what is robust across the sweep is a $\sim 7\times$ reduction in seed variance ($\sigma = 0.9$ vs.\ $6.4$ at 5\%). The architectural finding is that the \emph{causal} inductive bias dominates model complexity: a bidirectional encoder with VICReg memorises domain-specific patterns at the cost of frozen-probe generalisation, cross-machine transfer, and label efficiency.

On SMAP, the standard ``prediction error $=$ anomaly score'' reduction inverts sign on recurrent telemetry anomalies (sign gap $-0.61$, PA-F1 $0.22$ vs.\ MTS-JEPA's $0.34$). Re-scoring the \emph{same} frozen encoder with the Mahalanobis distance of $\h_\text{past}$~\citep{lee2018mahalanobis,sehwag2021ssd} at PCA rank $k=100$ lifts PA-F1 to $0.793 \pm 0.014$ on SMAP and $0.707 \pm 0.050$ on MSL across 3 pretraining seeds each - both substantially above MTS-JEPA's $0.336$. Against a random-init-encoder control at $k=10$, this decomposes into $0.588$ from scoring geometry alone and $+0.205$ from JEPA pretraining plus matched-rank covariance - the geometry contributes a substantial fraction, but pretraining adds a reproducible and larger-than-previously-reported increment on top. The ``one encoder, two probes'' framing is now justified: the same architecture and scoring recipe apply to both spacecraft benchmarks at matched PCA rank. We document a PCA-rank-mismatch failure mode at $k=10$ (MSL drops to PA-F1 $0.093 \pm 0.066$), and flag the hyperparameter sensitivity as a limitation rather than the method itself failing. A diagnostic decomposition further shows that 99.9\% of true-positive SMAP detections are continuation of an already-ongoing anomaly rather than anticipatory lead-time detections, so the ``grey swan \emph{prediction}'' framing of the paper does not extend to SMAP in its current form.

On the methodology side, under an honest probe protocol most of what we previously reported as ``architectural improvement from V2 to V17'' is a protocol fix rather than a real gain; the genuine SSL value is concentrated in the scarce-label regime.

Future work targets three directions: (i) closing the remaining gaps identified above - paired significance tests on matched engine-subset splits (the right test for the FAM-vs-STAR crossover, where the present unpaired test leaves the comparison n.s.\ at every budget), TS2Vec / PatchTST baselines on FD001, full label-efficiency sweeps on FD003/FD004, and a real-data (non-simulation) evaluation; (ii) a principled PCA-rank selection method for Mahalanobis-on-representation scoring, since the $7\times$ swing in MSL PA-F1 between $k=10$ and $k=100$ currently forces us to tune $k$ against labelled test data - and extending the representation-shift geometry to SWaT under the same recipe; and (iii) lead-time decompositions on datasets with graded precursor dynamics, since SMAP's continuation-dominated structure does not stress the prediction-in-advance axis.

\paragraph{Point-adjusted F1 controversy.}
The standard anomaly detection protocol~\citep{schmidl2022tsadeval} credits the entire anomaly segment if any single point is detected. This inflates scores to $>0.97$ on SWaT and PSM, saturating the leaderboard. We report non-PA F1 and AUC-PR as primary (realistic operational metrics) and PA-F1 for comparability with prior work. On SMAP, the Mahalanobis scoring (PCA-10 reference ablation, seed 42) lifts non-PA F1 from $0.04$ (L1) to $0.10$, PA-F1 from $0.22$ to $0.73$, and AUC-PR to $0.17$; at PCA-100 the 3-seed PA-F1 is $0.793 \pm 0.014$ on SMAP and $0.707 \pm 0.050$ on MSL (\cref{sec:anomaly_results}). The persistent gap between non-PA F1 and PA-F1 reflects that the detector fires at the wrong \emph{instants} within each anomalous window - a common failure mode for score-threshold detectors on contiguous anomaly segments, and one that point-adjustment masks. AUC-PR is the more honest threshold-free summary and is consistent with the non-PA F1 ordering across scorings.

\paragraph{NASA Scoring Function.}
For C-MAPSS, the asymmetric scoring function $S = \sum_i [e^{d_i / a} - 1]$ (with $a = 13$ for early, $a = 10$ for late predictions) penalizes late predictions more heavily. FAM E2E: NASA-S $= 395.7 \pm 69.4$ (5 seeds; FD001; RMSE $14.8 \pm 0.6$). Best seed: NASA-S $= 313.7$ (RMSE $= 14.4$). The high variance ($\pm 69.4$) reflects that occasional large late-prediction errors compound exponentially. 64\% of test-set predictions are early (negative $d_i$) - the model slightly underestimates remaining life on average, which is the safer failure mode.

\paragraph{Threshold exceedance metrics.}
We adopt the $\pm 3\sigma$ convention from statistical process control (SPC)~\citep{montgomery2020spc}, which provides a false alarm rate of 0.27\% under normality. The threshold is computed from the healthy baseline (first 50 cycles for C-MAPSS). The threshold exceedance probe protocol is described in \cref{sec:tte_results}; numerical results are future work.

\section{Cross-Sensor Attention Maps}
\label{app:attention_maps}

\paragraph{Visualization.} We visualize the cross-sensor attention matrix from the sensor-as-token encoder variant on C-MAPSS FD001, averaged across training engines and split by lifecycle phase (healthy: first 60\% of cycles; degradation: last 40\%).

During healthy operation, attention is diffuse across sensors. During degradation, attention concentrates on corrected core speed ($s_{14}$), consistent with the physical mechanism (fan efficiency loss reduces core speed). The top-5 healthy$\to$degradation attention shifts all point toward $s_{14}$: $s_3 \to s_{14}$ ($+0.103$), $s_{15} \to s_{14}$ ($+0.099$), $s_{11} \to s_{14}$ ($+0.096$).

This pattern is FD001-specific. On FD003 (fan + HPC faults), attention shifts in the opposite direction: the top degradation changes are defocusing from physical fan speed ($s_8$) and corrected core speed ($s_{13}$), while LPC outlet temperature ($s_3$) receives increased cross-sensor attention. Both findings are consistent with the underlying fault physics.

\textbf{Key insight:} the sensor-as-token architecture discovers physically meaningful sensor interactions \emph{without domain knowledge}---the sensor ID embeddings are learned, and the ordering is arbitrary. This supports the iTransformer-inspired design choice.
